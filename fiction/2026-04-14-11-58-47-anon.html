The Fifteen Glasses

By Anon 1,110 words 8.07/7

Prompt

A man has this dream. He calls about fifteen close friends for a getaway. When he comes back, the place is buckling with a hundred people he doesn't know. His friend Andrew tells him they already chose people to make decisions. Unfortunately, it's not him. He decides to leave but can't find his shoes under a pile of strangers' coats. NOTE: This is a dream. First person. The world operates on dream logic. Never say the word dream.

I counted the glasses twice. Fifteen is the kind of number that tricks you — three rows of five or five rows of three — and when you look at them on the counter they seem like more or fewer than they should be, depending on how you've spaced them. I spaced them with intention. Gaps where the bottles would go. Room for someone to set down a glass and pick it up again without touching the next one. The lamp I aimed at the doorway twenty minutes ago is still holding its angle, and my feet hurt from being on them all day, and still when I came home I moved the coffee table six inches to the left and walked the path a guest would walk and decided six inches was right. Six inches is the difference between turning sideways and not having to.

The door is open when I arrive and the music is not mine. The glasses are on the counter but someone has spread them out, filled the gaps I left between them. There are still fifteen. I count them to confirm this and confirm it and then I hear Andrew's voice from somewhere deeper in the apartment — not calling to me, just audible, the way a piece of furniture is audible if you move around it in the dark. I adjust the lamp. I put my limes in a bowl but the bowl is gone so I am holding limes in the middle of a room that holds a hundred people and I find Andrew near the kitchen because I need to set something down. He says my name — first name, warm, immediate — the voice of someone running a successful meeting. He gestures at the room and says, all this.

I tell the first stranger I'm not a guest, I live here. She says oh, of course, how lovely, and turns to the man beside her. I tell the second stranger the same thing. He says that's so great, this apartment has incredible flow. I say thank you, which is the wrong answer, and I know it's wrong when I say it. By the fifth person I have stopped explaining. Andrew touches my shoulder — brief, collegial — and turns to someone else mid-sentence. My hand finds the wall. The wall is where I expect it. The floorboards are creaking everywhere now, a hundred weights pressing down.

Near the kitchen again, later, Andrew explains. They felt it was best. A few people got together. He mentions a name I don't know. It's a committee name, or a person's name that sounds like a committee — flat, administrative, final. I say: this is my apartment. Andrew nods. The nod does not contest this. He turns toward someone behind him and I'm looking at his shoulder and then I'm looking at the room and then I'm looking at my bookshelf, which someone has rearranged. The books are in alphabetical order now. They were not in alphabetical order before. They were in the order of a person who reads — books beside the books they were read alongside, books from the same year grouped by accident. I put my hand against the wall. It is farther than I remember. My palm hangs in the air for a moment before it lands.

The closet is the last place left. The coats go from floor to rack and back to floor, dozens of them. I push through them toward the back. Brown leather shoes, worn at the heel, crease across the left toe — or the right toe, but one of them, I put the crease there myself over two winters. I find a brown shoe. I hold it in both hands and turn it toward the light. No crease. Or the crease is there and the light is wrong. I set it down. Nylon jacket. I push it aside. Leather, cold, stiff at the collar — not mine. Another nylon. A wool sleeve that does not belong to a coat I own. I push through it. A rubber sole, brick-red, too small. I set it aside. Canvas, navy, laces knotted in a way I would not knot them. I set it aside. A boot with a zipper. A loafer with a penny still in it. A shoe with no lace at all. I line them up along the closet floor, a row of wrong shoes.

I find a shoe with a crease. I hold it under the light and run my thumb along the toe. I look for its pair. I find a shoe that might be the pair except both have the same heel wear, or neither do, and I am not sure anymore which foot the crease belongs to. I press harder with my left when I write. I could prove it from first principles. Standing in the closet with someone's coat against my cheek I cannot remember which hand I write with. A woman says excuse me. She says it the way you say it when you mean it and I step back, I say sure, of course, and then I am in the hallway and the hallway is longer than it was and I am near the door.

The party continues at the exact volume and temperature it has maintained all night. People are laughing at something I didn't hear. Andrew is somewhere. The glasses — I can see three of them from here, scattered across surfaces, set down by hands that don't know their history. One of them is chipped. A small white crescent at the rim. I bought them at a hardware store, a cardboard box, chosen for being plain. I cannot remember if one was chipped when I opened the box. My feet are on the floor. The floor is where it was. The door is open. The building's hallway beyond it, the light that has always been slightly too yellow. The lamp I aimed at this doorway is doing its job on someone I don't know, lighting them from the angle I chose. The floorboards creak. My hand is against the wall. The creak of the floorboards is the sound of the floor now. The fifteen glasses are scattered and one of them is chipped and the light is doing the thing I planned for it to do and the six inches are exactly right — you can turn without turning sideways, you can move through the room, you can pick up a glass and set it down again without touching the one next to it.

I counted them twice. The number was correct. It still is.
