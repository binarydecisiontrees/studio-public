The Fish Cake Cooling

By Anon 1,090 words 8.07/7

Prompt

A family dinner where everyone knows the father is leaving but no one will say it. The sixteen-year-old is the one who finally does, and she's wrong about why.

The kalbi-jjim came out on a Tuesday. Soo-jin set her bag down by the door and smelled the braising liquid before she saw the pot, the deep brown smell of soy and pear and something rendered low for hours. Her mother had started it Sunday. She crossed the kitchen and lifted the lid and the short ribs had gone soft enough to pull apart with two fingers, the kind of soft that takes two days. On the counter: three side dishes in white bowls. Spinach pressed with sesame. Radish cut thin. Fish cake her mother had fried herself, the edges crimped and golden, cooling already on their plate. The rice cooker light read warm.

She sat at the table and her father was already there, phone face-down by his bowl.

Her brother poured himself water and asked about math. She said fine. He said his anyway. The refrigerator cycled and went quiet. Her father lifted his bowl and filled it from the communal pot and set the ladle back without resting it on the holder. A drop of broth landed on the table. Her mother watched it land.

Her father said nothing about the braising time. He chewed. He used to ask about the braising time.

Her mother set ribs on his plate.

Soo-jin lifted her chopsticks and the first piece of rib fell apart when she picked it up, meat separating cleanly from bone, and she thought about the receipt from the downtown restaurant on a Wednesday he'd supposedly worked through lunch. Lunch for two. She had seen the total — forty-seven dollars, not a solo lunch, not anywhere close — and she had folded it and put it back exactly where she found it, angled the same way in the junk drawer, because disturbing the evidence was something you didn't do. You cataloged. You waited.

Her brother asked for more rice. Her mother stood and filled his bowl. Her father's phone screen lit through the cloth, faint and white, and he pressed the side with his thumb without picking it up. His jaw shifted, a tightening and release, and then he was chewing again. Soo-jin put radish in her mouth. Crunched it. The sesame in the spinach was fresh — ground this morning, not from the container.

The screen stayed on for eleven seconds. She counted.

He asked about her brother's baseball. Her brother said they'd won, said the score. Her father nodded. He picked up another rib and had to chase part of it with the spoon. He used to do that without noticing. Now she noticed that he didn't notice.

Her mother lifted a piece of radish and ate it. Lifted another.

The undershirts had been the third thing. She had found them folded in his drawer, two of them, individual ones with paper tags still on, a brand she didn't know. Not the Costco packs. The tags were still attached because he hadn't worn them yet, or had taken them off and put them back, and she'd held one for a moment and felt the fabric — softer than his usual ones — and put it back exactly as she found it. Under the undershirts: nothing. She'd checked.

Her mother lifted a third piece of radish without looking at it. She was moving the way her father was moving — mechanically, processing the meal as task. The same rhythm. Soo-jin put her chopsticks down and picked them up again. The overhead light buzzed, the round white one, too bright, the kind of light that flattens everything to the same pale value.

Her mother did not ask how his day was.

Soo-jin waited for it. The question was as reliable as a grace before meals, how was your day, and her father would answer with a detail — one small clinical story from the pharmacy, a confused prescription, a regular customer who'd asked about something she recognized later as a chronic condition he was tracking by memory. He told those stories. She hadn't heard one in three weeks.

Her mother refilled his soup. Her father said thank you in the register you say thank you to a cashier.

The fish cake sat on its plate.

Soo-jin's mother knew. Not just an affair, but an affair being managed, absorbed, a two-day dish being cooked over it. The three side dishes. The ground sesame. You didn't cook like that in ignorance. You cooked like that when you needed your hands to be moving because otherwise they weren't.

Soo-jin set her chopsticks down on the rest. Picked them up. Put them in her lap.

He didn't back in tonight. His car pulled straight in, the usual way, two decades of straight in, and she had spent the walk to the door adjusting the case, because the reversed parking was part of the case, a man keeping the nose pointed out toward the street, and its absence required explanation. Maybe he'd forgotten. Maybe it meant he'd made a decision and no longer needed the exit. Her palm closed around both chopsticks and pressed them together, wood against wood, the grain biting slightly.

Her brother was asking about something, school or sports, she wasn't tracking it. The refrigerator kicked on.

Her mother reached for the ladle.

Soo-jin said: Is there someone else.

She said it to her father. The pitch didn't rise. Five words and then the sound of the refrigerator, which was louder than she'd realized.

Her father's fork stopped moving. Something went out of his face, or back — the bones behind it stepping away from the surface. He looked at his wife.

Her mother's hand stopped mid-reach, the ladle still in the pot, and lowered back to the table.

Soo-jin's case was built correctly. She had the receipt. She had the phone. She had the undershirts and the reversed car and the changed password and the silence where his stories used to be. She had assembled it without error, piece to piece, and the assembly was sound, and her father's face had just told her that all of it pointed somewhere else.

She didn't know where. His hand was flat on the table now, and her mother's hand was flat on the table, and Soo-jin pressed the chopsticks harder against her palm, the wood grain biting in, and looked at the fish cake on its plate.

Her mother picked up the pot with both hands and set it on the counter. The oil around the fish cake had started to cloud.

The rice cooker light still read warm.
