The Tupperware on the Sill

By Anon 1,171 words 8.67/7 ★★★★★

Prompt

A night-shift nurse describes the four hours between when a patient's family leaves for the night and when they come back in the morning. What happens in the room. What the machines sound like when no one is performing for them. What the patient does when they think no one is watching. What the nurse does when she knows no one is.

The chair vinyl is still warm. I press the back of my hand against it for a second, two, just to confirm what I already knew from the doorway — the shape of the visit is still in the room, the floral lotion smell, the tissue on the floor four inches from the wheel.

The Tupperware is on the tray table. Post-it on the lid, green, handwriting I haven't been able to read from the doorway and haven't needed to. I don't move it. I move past it and check his O2. Ninety-two. I write it down.

His hands are at his sides now. Left thumb against the gown hem — picking, picking — and I know from eleven days that he does this when he thinks someone might be watching and is trying to look like he isn't aware of it. His daughter stays for three hours and the thumb never moves. The moment she leaves it finds the hem.

"I'm going to silence the pump," I say. "It's going to alarm in a few minutes."

He nods without opening his eyes. The pump is at twelve. The bag is light. I've been watching it through the window for twenty minutes, the way it hangs. I know what light looks like.

I silence it at 1:47, two seconds before the alarm. I check the line. I flush it. I note the time. His lips move and I'm looking at the drip chamber, counting drops, because that is the thing I am doing right now, and somewhere behind the count — my father's hands cupped around a mug, breath blown into the space between his thumbs, the exhaled air going opaque and then gone — and I count drops until it passes.

His lips are still moving.

I don't ask. I check the rate. The math holds.

He needs the urinal at 2:31 and can't reach it and says I'm sorry before I'm even at the bed rail.

"Nothing to be sorry for," I say.

I assist. The motion has been mine for long enough that it's invisible to him because it's invisible to me.

He says thank you in a voice that means something other than thank you.

I say of course in a voice that means I heard it.

When I straighten his blanket his hand moves — not to his side. Under his chin. Fingers curled, cheek against the knuckle. I tuck the edge and check the IV drip rate. I check it again. The rate is fine. I checked it because his hand was under his chin and I needed to be looking at something with numbers.

I leave the room and eat a hard-boiled egg in the hallway in under ninety seconds. The shell goes in my pocket because there's no trash can within arm's reach and I've stopped caring about that.

At 3:22 I hear it before the monitor does.

The wet density in the bases — bilateral, both sides, different from midnight, which was bad, different from what I was expecting, which was worse than midnight but manageable. This is something else. I'm titrating the drip before the monitor updates, before I could explain to you how I knew, because the explanation would take longer than the knowing and he doesn't have the time I'd spend on it.

I page respiratory. My hands are doing what eleven years taught them. The Lasix order is already in.

He's watching my face.

Not frightened. Studious. The way you look at something when you're trying to read it before it reads you. I don't know which it is. I let that stand.

For four seconds I let him see me seeing him. I don't arrange my face. Then I look at the monitor.

The crackles don't resolve. They don't worsen. I stay longer than I have to, charting things I could chart from the station. The Tupperware is still on the tray table. He hasn't touched it. He can't touch it, NPO since Thursday, but someone drove it here anyway and set it where he could see it, and the Post-it is still on the lid, and what I notice now is that the Tupperware has a pattern on the side, small flowers, blue, the kind of container you buy for a picnic and keep for fifteen years, and this is the container someone reached for when they were deciding what to bring.

I go to the station and chart. I eat crackers over the sink because crumbs on scrubs end up in patient beds and I know this from years of finding crumbs in patient beds at the end of shifts that were not mine. Through the wall I can hear the plastic-on-metal creak of a bed — someone shifting weight, not the motion of distress, just the motion of trying to find the version of lying down that hurts less.

My father, at the end, made that sound.

I press my palms together and blow into the space between my thumbs. The air goes warm. My hands go warm. I go back in.

His eyes are open when I push the door with my hip.

The monitor at 88, lower than I want, but the crackles have been there for forty minutes and the Lasix needs time. I note the number. I look at his chest.

The Tupperware is exactly where I left it.

I'm at the tray table, moving the water cup two inches closer to him, and my hand goes to the Tupperware without deciding to. I lift it. It's heavier than it looks. Someone packed it full.

I set it on the windowsill. Not because it's in the way. Not because the tray table needs to be clear. I move the water cup two inches. I move the Tupperware to the sill, where the light will hit it in four hours, and where he can still see it but not from the angle that requires him to turn his head.

"The water's closer," I say.

He doesn't say anything. His thumb has stopped moving.

I check his O2. Eighty-eight. I check his skin — blanching at the fingertips, which I chart, which I have been charting all week. I adjust his blanket. His hand moves and I don't look at it and then I do — under his chin, curled there, the same gesture — and I tuck the edge of the blanket around his arm and my hands are warm from pressing them and I leave them there for one second, two, three.

Then I go to the far wall and write numbers on a piece of paper.

His thumb has stopped picking. The Post-it on the Tupperware catches the edge of the window light and I can read it now — two words, possibly three, I can see the shape but not the letters — and I decide this is not information I need.

His eyes are closed. His chest moves. I pull the next chart and write 3:58 AM.
