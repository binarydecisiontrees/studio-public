The Contractor's Estimate

By Anon 1,603 words 6.45/7

Prompt

A couple renovating their first apartment discovers that every decision — paint color, cabinet hardware, where the spice rack goes — is actually a proxy argument about something else entirely. The contractor figures this out before they do.

The door is quarter-sawn oak. 1920s, probably. Someone painted it twice — you can tell from the buildup at the hinge mortises — and someone else tried to strip it with a heat gun and quit halfway. The grain is coming through on the latch side where they got furthest. It's not a bad door. Needs new weatherstripping at the threshold, the top hinge is pulling out of softened wood, but the panel joints are tight. I've seen worse doors on houses that cost more.

I'm not thinking about the door yet. I'm thinking about the grout.

Forty minutes ago she said white and he said gray and she said white is cleaner and he said gray hides everything and she said that's the problem, and then they both looked at me like I was supposed to rule on it. I wrote "grout TBD" on the estimate sheet. I've been writing "TBD" a lot on this job. I have never written this many TBDs on a single estimate sheet. I'm running out of room in the margin.

They were mid-agreement when I arrived, which is how I knew it wasn't one. Yuna had the laptop open on the peninsula, turned so I could see the screen — subway tile, Sea Salt, three-by-six, a very reasonable choice. David said yeah, that works, his hand moving to the back of his neck. They finished each other's sentences when they explained it. The precision of people who rehearsed in the car.

The countertop is Calacatta quartz, the real stuff, tight veining, nothing like Carrara — Carrara moves, Calacatta holds. She picked it, or he agreed to it, or they picked it together: the story changes depending on who I'm standing closer to when I ask. The flooring is engineered white oak, five-inch plank, which they chose at the showroom while standing slightly too far apart for a couple choosing flooring together. You notice. Twenty-two years of kitchens and bathrooms, you start reading the distance between people the way you read the distance between studs. Sixteen inches on center, or not.

When I asked about grout she said white. He said gray. She said white is cleaner. He said gray hides dirt, in a voice that contained at least four other sentences underneath it. She said that's not really the point. He said what's the point then. She said white is fine too, right, in the voice of someone landing the plane. I wrote grout TBD. I have been on this job eleven days. I will be on it another six weeks. At this rate I will need a second estimate sheet just for TBDs.

They wanted to do cabinet pulls next, which is how we ended up with three samples on the counter — brushed brass, matte black, satin nickel — and Yuna saying she was flexible while eliminating each one. She's flexible. She said it three times. The brass was too warm, the black too stark, the nickel she just touched the edge of and set back down. David's phone buzzed and he looked at the screen and didn't say anything. He put it back in his pocket. Later I saw the screen as he unlocked it — his mother, a photo of some pull, the text probably about what they started with, what's wrong with what they started with. He scrolled past it. A man who scrolls past his mother's photo of a cabinet pull has made a decision about something larger than cabinet pulls.

I quoted the brass from memory: 4,200 won per unit, forty-six units, installed about 195,000. I didn't reach for the binder. I know my numbers. I knocked on the kitchen wall with my knuckle — the one between the kitchen and what used to be a dining room before someone put in a pass-through in the eighties and made a mess of it. The sound came back flat and definitive. I knocked on the adjacent wall. Hollow. "That one you can move," I said. "This one's holding up the floor above you." They were both quiet for a moment. Then Yuna said she'd think about the pulls.

We walked back to the bathroom, and the linoleum seam at the kitchen threshold — it's getting ripped out, all of it, but for now it's still there — made two different sounds under them. Yuna steps over it on the left side, David on the right. The left side has a little give. The right side doesn't. I have no professional reason to know this.

The paint boards were already leaning against the tub surround: Accessible Beige, Agreeable Gray, Alabaster. Whoever names paint colors has a strong opinion about the letter A. In direct sun they look identical. In the afternoon light coming through the poly sheeting they pull apart — the beige goes yellow, the gray goes blue-green, the white goes warm. David said his parents used semi-gloss in every room. Semi-gloss is easy to clean, he said. Yuna was quiet for exactly the amount of time it takes to pick up a paint board and set it down again. She said she'd read that satin was better for walls over a certain age. The walls are plaster, 1920s, they'll take either. I said they'd take either.

Then they were both talking and neither was finishing a sentence. I'm not saying — You literally just — That's not what — The bathroom door, stripped to bare wood on one side, sat between them like a referee who'd given up. Yuna said it's not about the money. David went quiet in the specific way that contains an entire paragraph. I wrote something on my clipboard. It was not a measurement. It was a very small drawing of a door.

Satin has a sheen level of about 25 to 35 degrees. Semi-gloss runs 55 to 70. The practical difference is that semi-gloss holds up better in wet areas and wipes clean. This is the only thing I am qualified to say about sheen.

She said it again, it's not about the money, and I wrote down the dimensions of the bathroom window, which I already had.

I said lunch, and capped my pencil, and the click was the loudest sound in the room. No one argued with the click.

The stoop was cold. I had a triangle kimbap from the GS25 two blocks down, tuna mayo, which I bought because they were out of the spam and egg. I ate it in four bites. A woman walked a dog that was wearing a jacket nicer than mine. A delivery bike jumped the curb and didn't slow down. None of these things were about grout. I folded the kimbap wrapper into a square, edges matched, and put it in my jacket pocket and stood up.

When I came back in they were in the kitchen, and something had changed in how they were standing — not closer, but facing the same direction. David was through the doorframe and not in it. That's the thing I noticed. He was through it.

They asked about the kitchen wall — could it come down, really? For the first time all day the question was about the wall. Yuna had her arms crossed but loosely, and David had his hands in his back pockets, and they were both looking at the wall the way you look at something you're deciding to change.

I knocked. Listened. Hollow along the whole run of it, studs set wide, no beam work overhead, nothing structural within three meters. "Partition," I said. "It can come down."

David asked what was behind it. I said probably a lot of old wiring and some mice that moved out in the nineties. Yuna laughed, which was the first real sound I'd heard all day. David smiled at the wall. The wall did not smile back, but it was a partition wall, so it was in no position to be difficult about anything.

I unfolded the dust sheet from the sawhorse — six-by-four, poly-cotton, mine — and refolded it the way I do at the end of every job day. Not because it needs it, because it doesn't, but because the fold tells me where I am: first fold, edge to edge; second fold, thirds; third fold, square, press. It takes about forty-five seconds. It is the most decisive thing that happens in some houses. I picked up my coffee from the windowsill. It was cold. I drank it anyway. Cold coffee is still coffee. The joint compound smell was still in the room — wet plaster, the smell that gets into everything and lingers two weeks after you're done.

The estimate sheet had seven TBDs and one small drawing of a door. The renovation will cost them forty-three million won — materials, labor, the bathroom door if they restore it, which they should, because that's old-growth oak and you cannot buy it anymore, not for what it would cost to replace it with something that looks like it. My wife gets home at six-thirty. I promised perilla leaves. I've revised the estimate three times. Each time, more TBDs.

Tuesday I'd fix the baseboard gap — the one at the bedroom door, the one I said I'd get to and hadn't yet, a small failure of fit between two things that should have met and didn't quite. I'd bring the right filler. I'd take my time with it. The filler comes in thirty-seven colors. I would not be asking them to choose.

The door is quarter-sawn oak and it will outlast the argument about the grout.
