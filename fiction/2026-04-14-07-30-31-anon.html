The Soft Spot

By Anon 1,135 words 8.77/7 ★★★☆☆

A child asks an AI assistant why the sun goes down. The AI gives the scientific answer. The child says that's not what they meant.

The cup is not warm. Has not been warm for twenty minutes. She holds it anyway, fingers laced, thumbs on the rim, and drinks from the same spot without thinking about it.

Marcus is on the floor between her feet and the kitchen island. He has two crayons — one in each fist, the way he still holds them. The phone bill is mostly his now. In the margins, in his tight spiral, a house takes shape. She counts the windows without meaning to.

Six. She had counted them already. Seven before, but he colored one shut.

The blinds are level. She checked when he dragged the little speaker into the kitchen, the one she bought him at Target. She pretended to look at the spider plant, the one her mother started from a cutting eight years ago. Beside it, the socket wrench. She has stopped seeing the socket wrench.

She takes another drink from the cup. Cold.

"Hey," Marcus says, not to her. To the speaker. "Why does the sun go down?"

Her shoulders ease. Ten more minutes.

The assistant's voice: unhurried, patient. The Earth spins. As it spins, your part of it turns away from the sun. The horizon is like the edge of a table — once the sun crosses it, you can't see it from where you're standing.

"Oh," Marcus says. He is quiet for a moment. His crayons go still. Then: "That's not what I meant."

She looks at the back of his head. The little bump at the base of his skull where his hair makes a whorl. She knows that bump the way she knows the soft spot near the sink — has mapped it in the dark.

He turns. Not at the speaker.

At her.

She could say: The light gets tired.

She could say: Because it always has.

She could say: I don't know, bug, what did you mean?

Her left hand moves. Two fingers finding the left side of her face, tucking her hair back. Then the right side, the same motion, same two fingers.

She opens her mouth.

"Anyway," she says.

Nothing follows it. The word just stands there in the kitchen like something she put down and can't pick back up. His face stays open in the patient specific way he has, the way he looks when he is waiting for her to continue and is certain she will because she always does.

The refrigerator compressor runs. She has been registering it all evening, running and stopping and running again.

Forty-three.

She sets the cup down.

She sets it down on the counter near the sink, the wrong place, and she knows it is the wrong place the way you know a stair in the dark, and the cup comes down on the soft spot in the linoleum and makes a sound.

Hollow. The sound the floor has always made there. Has made for three years, since the winter after Marcus was born, since the pipes did something she had a man come look at who shook his head.

She has stepped around that spot for three years. A small correction, automatic.

Until now, when she hears the sound.

She cannot see what her face does but she can feel the moment it happens — a small collapse somewhere below her eyes, above her mouth. The cup has ended up back in her hand. She finds her position: fingers laced, thumbs on the rim.

Marcus says, Mom.

Not a question.

The light through the blinds has finished thinning. The wall is dark, the lines gone, the car-shadows no longer moving because there are no longer cars to make them.

Her left hand finds her hair again. She lets it.

He is still looking at her. Both crayons are down. His hands are on his knees, small and still.

The assistant's light pulses once, blue, and goes still.

In the evenings, after he sleeps, she asks it things. The capital of Peru. How long a caterpillar takes to become a butterfly. Whether you can refreeze salmon that has been thawed. She knows the answers. Knows she knows them before she asks. What she needs is the voice: thorough, without hurry, not saying I know you're scared, but—

She asks it questions and it answers and the room has a third presence in it and the third presence doesn't want a single thing from her.

Marcus is still looking.

The Walgreens on Eighth, the one near the taco place, the Benadryl, not something for his cough, always the exact bottle, the blue one. She can feel a lie forming in the back of her throat two seconds before she tells it.

Nothing is forming.

"The Earth spins," she says.

He waits.

"And we're on one part of it. And the sun only reaches some parts at a time."

"I know that," Marcus says. "She told me that."

"What did you mean, then," she says. Not a question.

He picks up the brown crayon. He looks at his phone bill house, the one that has six windows now, was seven.

"I meant why does it have to," he says.

The refrigerator runs.

She is standing next to the soft spot now. Not on it. Her feet found their correction again without asking her.

The wrench on the windowsill. At some point it arrived. At some point it became furniture. She cannot make herself ask.

"It doesn't want to," she says.

Marcus looks up.

"The Earth doesn't want to spin. It just does. And every time the sun goes down, it's not going anywhere. It's just that the Earth moved. You'll see it again."

He looks at her the way he looks at her sometimes.

She does not look somewhere else.

He goes back to drawing. The crayon moves in circles first, a foundation, then up. Walls. She watches him build it.

No windows.

She watches him not put windows in it. She drinks from the cup — from the wrong spot, the two o'clock is not where her mouth lands — and the cold goes down and the refrigerator compressor runs and stops and the apartment does what it does in the dark.

She does not check the blinds.

She is standing close enough that she could, that the reflex is there, that her hand knows the distance. The latch, the tilt rod, the level she spent five months getting right because one bracket was two degrees off and she could feel it.

The blinds are level. She knows this. She checked at 6:47.

She does not check again.

He draws his house. The wrench sits on the sill inside its ring of rust. The spider plant is alive.

The refrigerator stops.
