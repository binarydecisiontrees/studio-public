The Yeosu Pioneer

By Anon 1,545 words 8.37/7

The engine hum is in Sohee's molars. She has been clenching. She does not know for how long.

There is a ship. She sees it the way she sees anything she needs to verify — twice, both times counting. The hull is rust-red below the waterline where the gray begins, and the gray is broad and permanent. Above it the container stacks stand orange and blue and one red in the second row from the left, and she counts seven and her eyes go back to the first stack and she counts again and it's seven. The name is on the hull in white letters, the kind of font where no one decided on it — it was simply always that font, the default, the given: YEOSU PIONEER. She reads it. She reads it again. The wake behind the ship is open. She has been watching it since before she decided to watch it, two lines spreading out from the stern at maybe fifteen degrees from centerline, widening and widening into the gray-green water, and they don't come back together.

Dojin is beside her at the rail. His jacket carries the cabin smell — recycled air and vinyl seating — though underneath those layers the smell that is his, the particular staleness of his coats that she has learned to locate by subtracting what surrounds it. He is drinking coffee from a paper cup with a lid that doesn't fit right and he keeps pressing it down with his thumb. Click. The lid gives and catches. Jaw loose. Lips barely parted. A face with no work to do.

She says, "That one's heading to Busan, maybe." The throwaway register.

His head tilts toward her, then back to the water.

"The cargo ship," she says. "Twelve, fifteen kilometers. Maybe more."

He looks at the water.

She watches his eyes. She knows how Dojin's focus changes when it catches something — a small adjustment, barely visible, the way a hand closes differently around something real than around nothing at all. He is looking where she's pointing. The adjustment does not come.

"There," she says. Her arm still slightly raised from pointing.

He follows her finger. He looks — she can see the effort in the slight narrowing of his eyes, the small forward tilt of his head. He keeps looking. His expression holds the quality of open water.

The click of the lid.

She puts both hands on the rail. The draft of the ship is deep — she can see how much of the hull sits below the surface, the rust-red going down farther than she expected, which means it left somewhere full, it left carrying everything it could carry. The bridge structure at the bow end is white, three stories, the windows dark from this distance. A piece of foam drifts through the left line of the wake, crosses into open water and keeps going.

Dojin says, "Seagull?" Without certainty, not really waiting.

She looks. She can't tell from here. She looks back at the ship, and when she looks back at Dojin he is already looking somewhere else.

She is looking somewhere else too, now — Wolpo Beach, the jellyfish. Lung, she said. Bag, he said. She laughed because it was funny, two people, one thing, two names. But bag is in her mouth now, flat, plastic, and she is trying to locate what went into his eyes before the word came out — the seeing that preceded the naming, the moment when it was still just shape and translucence before language arrived and closed it down. She cannot locate it. She has never been able to locate it. The apartment in Mapo, first walkthrough, the 4pm light through the south windows, amber on the floor, the reason she said yes. She has never asked what he saw when he looked at those windows. What was in his ocean before she named the ship.

The ship is still there. She can see the flag at the stern now, red and blue on white, small, moving slightly with the ship's own passage through the air. The crane arm on the port side is angled down at maybe forty degrees, its hook obscured by angle but its geometry clear, a tool at rest. The rust below the waterline mark is not uniform — it branches from a central mass in thin irregular lines, a growth pattern, something that spread over years in salt water and no one drew. The two rear container stacks are blue but a different blue from the other blue stacks, dirtier or older or both, a shade she can distinguish if she squints, which she is doing. She is still doing it.

She looks at Dojin. He is pressing the lid. Click. His face is in the thin cloud-light, flat and even, and he is looking at the water.

"Rust-red at the waterline," she says. "Seven stacks — the rear two are a different shade of blue. The bridge is white, three levels, dark windows. The port-side crane arm is angled down. There is a flag at the stern."

She says it the way she would read an incident report.

Dojin puts his coffee cup on the rail ledge. He turns and looks where she's pointing, both eyes, sustained attention, and she watches — three seconds, four, five, six — and nothing in his face arrives.

He says, "The light."

She looks.

Between here and the horizon, maybe ten degrees south of the ship, a break in the cloud cover lets through a column of different light, silver-white against the flat gray, hitting the surface of the water and making it almost luminous, just there, in that one spot.

He tilts his chin toward it. "You see it," he says. Not a question. The tone he uses for things that simply are — the lock's engaged, it's not raining yet.

She sees it. The light is real. And the thought comes without warning, a single seam opening: what if I. What if the ship is the thing that isn't there and the light is what they share and she has been pointing at nothing — what if. She holds the seam open for one second, two, measuring it. The YEOSU PIONEER is on its heading, loaded, the flag still moving, the crane arm at its rest angle, the hull deep in the water, and she has been reading this ship with the same methods she trusts for everything and the methods are not wrong. The seam closes. But she felt it open.

And Dojin is pointing at genuine light in an ocean that does not have a ship in it.

He reaches into his coat pocket without looking. He peels tangerines in advance, before standing somewhere for a while — the smell of tangerine on open water. He hands her a segment without looking at her. She takes it. The acid hits first, then underneath it the sweetness. He eats one beside her, quietly, and the peel is curling in his left hand, and the ship holds its heading, and she watches the wake spread and spread and not come back together.

She says nothing about the ship.

Below deck the porthole is a gray circle and the cabin is small. Dojin lowers the folding table and uses it for his shoes and his book and the empty paper cup, lid slightly ajar. He unties one shoe. It drops: one sound, clean, specific, one thing. She hears it and he hears it. It is the same sound.

She checks the cabin lock. She hears the catch. Four seconds later she checks it again — her thumb wants the confirmation again and her thumb already has the confirmation and she gives it what it wants and it's still locked and it was locked both times.

She sits on the bunk. She is thinking about the hull. The branching rust below the waterline, the growth no one planned. The flag she moved from inferring to seeing. The porthole row she found only because she kept looking. She is thinking about Dojin saying you see it with the certainty of a person who has never had to doubt his own ocean.

She checks the lock. The handle under her thumb has a specific shape, a raised lip on one side, worn down a little from how many times it has been pressed. The answer is the same as the last time and the time before and her thumb knows this and she checks it anyway and it's locked and locked and it is still locked and she holds the shape of the handle without pressing, her thumb reading the worn lip, and then she presses, and it's locked.

Dojin has his paperback open. The spine is creased at the back third. She doesn't know what happens in the back third of that book.

The engine hum is in her teeth. The porthole is a gray disk. Out there the YEOSU PIONEER is on its heading, loaded, rust-red hull going deep into the water, wake still spreading behind it in two lines that will keep widening until the distance makes them invisible. Her thumb is on the handle. She knows it's locked. She checks it anyway.
