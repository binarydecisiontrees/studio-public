The Faucet Drips

By Anon 1,126 words 8.47/7

Prompt

Nadia sets her father's phone face-down on the kitchen table between them. She doesn't say what she found. He's watching her the way he used to watch her at recitals — that expression she always read

The coffee maker is still ticking through its cool-down. 4:56. She has been here nine minutes.

Her father is at the stove. His back is wide and she knows the geography of it — the slight left-list from thirty years of a violin on that shoulder — but she is looking at the orange-handled carafe on the counter instead, the way the west light catches the chrome collar, making it look overexposed, clinical, like a photograph taken with too much flash. She pours herself water. She has ice in her water.

He asks if she's hungry.

She says no.

He adjusts the lid on the pot. A quarter-inch correction and then his hand stays there a moment before lifting. She watches the hand. Something in her stomach moves. She puts her water glass down on the tabletop and watches the condensation press out between glass and surface, five small windows forming and dissolving.

The faucet drips. Three-second gap, then four, then two. She keeps waiting for a pattern to establish itself.

He turns around. His face does something — a configuration she has seen for twenty-eight years and has always read one way, which is: I am assessing this carefully. He sits down across from her and his hands go around his mug. The grip — three fingers on the left, two on the right, thumbs aligned — is the grip she watched from a folding chair in the Whitmore recital hall at nine and again at sixteen, when he stood at her music stand between movements and straightened the page. The page was already straight.

She starts the arithmetic again. She can't not. Fourteen transfers, varying amounts, averaged at $1,145 per, which is $16,030 total, which is what she arrived at in the driveway before coming inside and which will change if she finds more going back, and she might find more going back, and the number keeps shifting its meaning the way the expression on his face keeps shifting its meaning now that she is reading it with the new information applied retroactively, which is not fair to either the expression or the number but is what is happening.

He says something about the neighbor's elm tree. The roots, the sidewalk. She says mmm. He has talked about this tree before, she thinks — or another tree, in another year. His cuticles are ragged. They were never ragged. She is noticing this for the first time, which means either they became ragged recently or she has been looking at his hands wrong for years.

The faucet. Four seconds. Two. Two.

She is back at the recital. Third movement, the shift into third position, the string crossing she had practiced until the callus on her fourth finger split and she practiced through the split because she did not want his face to do the assessment expression during her movement. She had looked up mid-phrase and his face was doing it anyway, and she had taken it into her chest like a held note and carried it for seventeen years as he was proud and working not to show it because that was the story she needed it to be, and now the recital memory will not hold its shape, it keeps sliding when she presses on it, the expression won't stay still under the new interpretation or the old one, and the problem with the new interpretation is that it might not be right either, it might be a third thing she doesn't have a name for yet.

She knows what she saw on the phone. She knows the name she didn't recognize. She knows the number — of dates in the notification history she did not mean to see. She opens her mouth.

He says her name. Just her name. Nadia-jaan. In the register he uses when she was fourteen and left her violin in the rain, when she was twenty-two and called him from outside the bar because she couldn't drive. She closes her mouth. The phone is between the sugar bowl and the napkin holder, face-down, green case. A pulse of light leaks through. She does not look down. She presses her thumbnail into the pad of her ring finger and keeps it there.

Her father is still looking at her. Not at the phone. At her. His mug is in both hands and the steam has stopped rising and she is about to walk through it when she hears the sound of tires on gravel. Too early. She knows the engine. The loose heat shield, the particular rattle on deceleration that Daniel — her husband — has been promising to fix since December. She counts. One. Two. Three. Four. Five.

She can see her father's eyes move toward the hallway. She does not let her eyes follow. She keeps them on him. His hand is still on the mug. His other hand moves to the salt shaker — barely a touch. The car door shuts. She says nothing.

The front door opens. Daniel's voice — hey, sorry I'm late, the 405 — and he is dropping keys, doing the thing with his jacket, and her father turns toward him and says sit, it's almost ready, and Daniel comes into the kitchen and doesn't know to look at her. She picks up her water glass. The faucet drips. Daniel is talking about the construction. Her father answers. She watches her father's face while he talks to Daniel and it rearranges into something she recognizes — the listening expression — and she cannot tell if this is the face she built her understanding of him on for twenty-eight years or if this is a different face she is now reading with the old key.

He laughs at something Daniel says. The one with the inhale. This laugh is real.

She looks at his hands. The cuticles. The salt shaker is a quarter-inch from where it started. She looks at her phone, face-down between the sugar bowl and the napkin holder, and she does not flip it over. Daniel asks if there's bread. Her father gets up to check. She is alone at the table for four seconds. Her hand rests near the phone. She does not touch it. She picks up her water glass and sets it down in exactly the same place, and outside the west light is gone now, the overexposed clinical brightness is gone, and the kitchen is a kitchen again — her father's kitchen, the elm tree and the pot on the stove and the ragged cuticles — and her father comes back with the bread and sets it on the table and she looks at his face and tries to read it the old way.

The faucet drips.
