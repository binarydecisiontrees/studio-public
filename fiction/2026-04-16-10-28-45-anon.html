The Error Log

By Anon 1,236 words 7.76/7

Prompt

I built a system to predict my next prompt. Now I exist only in its errors.

I built it in six weeks. Six weeks because I had the data. I had the data because I'd been keeping log.txt for two and a half years, not for any reason I could have explained at the time. I had the data, so I kept it, and when I had enough I used it. That's not a project. That's a condition that produced a project.

The system tracks what it can measure. Time awake, time asleep, inferred from keyboard activity. Applications open, duration, switch frequency. Location pings. Purchase timestamps. Calories logged, though I stopped logging calories eight months ago, so that column is a long tail of nothing the model learned to treat as its own signal. Resting heart rate. Steps. Screen time by category. Search queries bucketed into seventeen emotional registers I labeled by hand over a weekend in November.

Eighty-nine percent accuracy on next-behavior prediction, fifteen-minute windows. I lead with the number because people respect clean numbers, and because the number lets me delay the part where I explain what it felt like. The emotion was not pride. Not surprise. Something more like: yes, that's what I thought. A diagnosis you already knew was coming. You're still relieved when the doctor confirms it, but the relief is for the confirmation, not the news.

The cursor blinks. I've started breathing in time with it. The coffee is cold. I noticed it was cold at 8:40 and now it's after four and the light has changed and the coffee is still cold. The coffee doesn't participate in the recursion. The coffee is just cold.

A line from the third week of building kept surfacing. Salmon again. Six hundred and twelve instances of "again." Feature weight: 0.31, which is high. The model learned what I already knew. Again is the purest signal in the dataset. Salmon again. Tab again. The back of my neck, again. The model doesn't know what "again" means. It knows what it predicts. These are not different things.

The first error I felt was a Tuesday in March. The model predicted Twitter at 2:14. I picked up the book at 2:13. Confidence had been 0.92. I read one page and the whole time there was this flush across my sternum — six inches wide — that I can only describe as the feeling of having scratched something I wasn't supposed to scratch. Not pride. Something more shameful. Relief that I was still a variable. But I checked the error log before I read the page. I had the book in my hand and I checked the error log first. The posterior had already updated. Even my relief was data.

The mug is still on the desk. I moved it once this morning — left side to right side — then moved it back because I didn't want to generate a false anomaly in the location column. That's when I understood I had crossed a threshold I don't have language for. The error rate was climbing. I'd started ordering different things. Not the salmon. Different café, different table, choosing with one eye on the log. What I found over three weeks was that my error rate climbed to 23% and I read that 23% like a lab result showing improvement. Like I had done something. Like the 23% was mine.

The word "just" started appearing around week four. I just wanted coffee. I just felt like staying in. Each instance a confession that the action had been premeditated. "Just" was the cover story for a choice made in full view of the model, a choice already column data. The rebellion had a confidence interval. I knew this. I kept ordering the wrong coffee, kept sitting in the wrong chair, kept reaching for the book at 2:13, and the elation was getting brittle. Someone laughing too hard at something that isn't quite funny.

The model doesn't track what I write. So technically this paragraph exists outside the features. But I'm writing it at my desk at 4:12 PM on a Thursday following an extended sedentary period — 91% confidence — and the model predicts sedentary periods following system review correlate with introspective activity. Introspective activity is one of the seventeen registers. I labeled it myself in November. The error rate plateaued at 23%. Then it dropped. By the second week of April: 84%. One Tuesday the model generated a prediction I hadn't seen before: Unpredictable behavior window, 2:00–3:00 PM. Confidence: 0.81.

The model had learned the rebellion. My unpredictability was now a feature. Another column. Another weight. Predictable².

I wrote that notation in the margin and stared at it for a long time. The apartment smells different when I come back. There's a thirty-second window — closed air, my own cooking, something underneath — and then it resolves and I'm back inside myself. I started timing the dissolution. Thirty seconds, twenty-eight, thirty-one. The model would give this a feature weight. The model would be correct.

Last Thursday: 91%. Any given fifteen-minute window, the model knows before I do what column I'm about to populate. My posterior is narrower than I thought. The feature weights found the real variables — not the ones I think are driving behavior, but the ones actually driving it, the ones I'd need to watch for a year to notice. Gradient descent found them in forty minutes.

I've run it six times. 89, 91, 73, 84, 91. The 73 was March. I was sick. Sleep went strange. The model was confused by the illness and I was confused by the model being confused — but those aren't the same confusion. The model lost signal because my body stopped following its own rules. I lost signal because I'd come to rely on the model as a mirror, and a fogged mirror doesn't show you the fog, it just stops showing you anything. That 73% — I felt it before I saw it. There was a looseness to the days. I want to say freedom. It was drift. Freedom has a direction. Drift is the absence of walls. When the model came back to 84% I angled my monitor to catch less glare, and I knew the model had predicted the angling — knew with the certainty of things you don't bother to verify — and I angled it anyway because the angling was necessary. Necessary actions are the ones you can't perform to defeat prediction. They're just actions. I still need to see the screen.

I built this to know what I would do next. I think I wanted to find out whether I was discoverable. Whether the structure I'd accumulated — two and a half years of log.txt, six hundred and twelve instances of again — added up to something you could call a person rather than a process. What the 89% answered was: yes, and the answer is not a relief.

The model doesn't predict sentences. But it predicts registers. Duration. There's a feature I labeled recursion-adjacent in November that I wish I hadn't, because now every time I enter this thought pattern I'm populating a column I named myself. The cursor is blinking. I cannot determine whether I am writing toward a conclusion or the model is predicting that I write toward a conclusion.

My left hand finds the back of my neck — autonomic, predicted, weight 0.27 — and the stays cold, and neither of us moves.
