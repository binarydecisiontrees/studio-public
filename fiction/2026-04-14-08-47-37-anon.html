The Account Holder

By Anon 1,652 words 8.36/7

Prompt

A man spends three hours on hold with his dead wife's phone company trying to cancel her plan. The hold music is a song she loved. He keeps getting transferred. Each representative asks him to verify her identity.

He dials at 8:03.

The phone rings twice and then a voice tells him his call is very important to them and asks him to stay on the line. He stays on the line. The hold music is something with piano and no lyrics. He reads the account number off the legal pad again, even though he's memorized it. He is in the chair by the window, not the comfortable one. He chose this one without knowing why and now he knows: it faces the door to the bedroom, which is closed, and this is easier than the couch, which faces the bookshelf.

A woman picks up. Young-sounding, efficient. "Thank you for calling Verizon, my name is Ashley, how can I help you today?"

"My wife passed away," he says. He hears himself say it the way you hear yourself on a voicemail — not quite your voice, slightly smaller, played back at the wrong speed.

"I'm so sorry for your loss." Fast. Professional. "I'll need to verify the account before I can make any changes. Can I get the account holder's name?"

He gives it.

"And the PIN?"

"Zero five two three," he says. He types it into the keypad without thinking, the way you type a word you've spelled a thousand times.

"Last four of the social?"

He reads it from the legal pad.

"One moment while I pull up the account." A click. The music again. He takes one sip of coffee and sets the mug down on the counter. The sip is too hot. He won't notice until much later. The mug says Property of No One in faded serif, a gift from her sister that she kept because she liked the disclaimer.

Ashley comes back. "Mr. Park, I've verified the account, but for cancellations I'm going to need to transfer you to our Account Services team. Is that okay?"

He says yes. It is 8:41.

The second representative picks up in four minutes. Marcus. Older voice, unhurried.

"Thank you for holding, my name is Marcus, can I get the name on the account?"

David tells him.

"And you're the account holder's—"

"Spouse," he says. "She passed away. I need to cancel the account."

"I'm very sorry for your loss." A beat before it, a beat after. "I'll need to verify the account before we can proceed. Can I get the PIN?"

He types it. "Zero five two three."

"And the last four—"

"Nine four one two." Before Marcus finishes.

A pause. "Thank you. One moment while I pull that up."

He is on hold again. The same piano. It takes four bars and then he knows. His hand moves to the mute button and stays there, not pressing. He breathes through it. It's a song she hummed sometimes in the car, something the algorithm had served her and she'd loved it the way she loved things — quickly, decisively, without explanation — she'd say this one, yes and add it to a playlist and that was that. He takes his hand off the mute button.

He shifts the phone to his right hand. His left ear is already warm.

Marcus comes back. "Mr. Park, I apologize, but account cancellations need to go through our Cancellation Department, which is a separate team."

He laughs. From the stomach, with no audience. It dies in the room.

"Okay," he says.

"Is there anything else I can help you with before I transfer you?"

He says no. He stares at the rectangle he drew inside the rectangle on the legal pad.

The third representative's name is Priya. She sounds careful, precise.

"Thank you for your patience. Can I get the name on the account?"

He gives it.

"And your relationship to the account holder?"

"Husband." He corrects himself a half-second later, silently, in his body.

"And you're calling to cancel the account?"

"Yes."

"I'll need to verify the account. Can I get the PIN?"

He types it. And then: "Zero five two three. May ninth, two thousand and three. Our first date. She used it for everything — the phone, the debit card, the gym locker. She said if someone loved you enough to guess all your passwords, they'd earned it." He stops.

Priya waits one beat. "Thank you," she says. "Can I also get the last four of the social?"

He gives it.

She asks for the date of birth.

"March ninth, 1987."

He has said it before, on forms, to doctors, in the years when he'd had to speak for her in waiting rooms and she'd squeeze his arm to indicate he'd gotten it right. Now he says it in a kitchen where she is not, at speaking volume, to a stranger.

"Mother's maiden name?"

"Yoon."

Typing. He works his thumbnail under the lifted corner of the screen protector without deciding to. The corner has been lifted since September and she kept meaning to replace it and did not.

"I'm showing this account as eligible for cancellation. Can you confirm the billing address?"

He confirms it. He hears himself confirm it. He is saying the address where she lived with him. He is saying it correctly.

His own sigh plays back through the receiver. A feedback loop, some glitch in the system — his own breath amplified. He says, to no one in the kitchen: "Even the phone company thinks I need to calm down." He laughs. The same laugh, stomach muscles, no one watching. It dies faster this time.

Priya says: "I apologize for the hold, Mr. Park. I'm going to need to transfer you to Cancellations Processing to complete this."

"You're not the Cancellations Department."

"I'm in Cancellations Support," she says. "Processing is a separate queue. I'm sorry for the inconvenience."

He does not say anything. He looks at the parallelogram of light on the floor, which has been shrinking since 9 AM. He picks the callus on his thumb.

The fourth representative is Diane. He can hear it in her voice — she's been doing this a long time.

"Hello, this is Diane with Verizon Cancellations Processing, can I get the name on the account?"

He gives the name.

"And am I speaking with Mrs. Park today?"

He starts to correct her. He opens his mouth. One hour and forty-one minutes in, and he has said the sentence three times and each time it has cost something, and he starts to say no, I'm her husband, and then he stops. He says: "Yes."

The woman on the line is talking to his wife. He listens.

"—for security purposes I'll need to verify a few things. Can I get the PIN?"

"Zero five two three," his wife says, through his mouth.

"Last four of the social?"

"Nine four one two."

"Date of birth?"

March ninth. 1987. He says it.

Diane types. He needs to use the bathroom since hour two. He heard a story once about someone losing their place in a queue after nine hours on hold with the IRS and he does not know if this story is true and it does not matter because now it is the thing that keeps him in the chair.

"Mr. Park." Diane's voice is slower than the others. She picks her words up before she says them, examines them. "I'm sorry for your loss."

He can't answer. His throat closes. He presses mute and holds it. Six seconds. Seven. He breathes. He unmutes.

"Thank you," he says.

She processes it. More typing. She confirms the billing address. She asks if he has the account holder's phone in his possession and he says yes, which is not precisely true. There are things in the apartment he has not been able to approach directly. She says she'll send a confirmation to the email on file and the final bill will be prorated.

"Is there anything else I can help you with before I put you on a brief hold for final processing?"

He says no.

The hold music starts. The same piano. No lyrics. Two notes, and his diaphragm locks up the way a circuit breaker trips: not a sob, not a breath, a seize. He presses mute so fast the phone slips in his hand and he catches it. He is sitting in the chair that faces the bedroom door with his right hand cramped around a purple silicone phone case and the music plays through him the way water finds the shape of whatever contains it and fills it.

It was just a song she played in the kitchen. He doesn't know what she was making. A regular dinner. A Tuesday. The phone propped against the backsplash, the algorithm serving her something, and she'd added it without ceremony and it had become the sound of ordinary Tuesday. He was somewhere in the apartment. He could hear it. He did not stop to think about it.

The music plays. The parallelogram of light on the floor is nearly a line now. He is here and she is not and the song plays to its end.

Diane comes back.

"Mr. Park, the cancellation has been processed. You'll receive a confirmation email within 24 to 48 hours. Is there anything else I can help you with today?"

He says: "No. Thank you."

"Thank you for being a Verizon customer, and again, I'm truly sorry for your loss."

"Thank you," he says again. There is nothing to thank her for except that she was careful.

He hangs up.

The kitchen is quiet. The line of light on the floor holds still. The coffee is cold in the mug. One sip taken. The legal pad has the account number at the top, below it a rectangle inside a rectangle, below that nothing. His right hand cramps open slowly, releasing the shape of the phone.

He sits in the chair that faces the bedroom door, which is closed.

He does not move from it.
