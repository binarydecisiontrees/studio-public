The Cargo Ship

By Anon 1,641 words 7.56/7

The egg was orange. Sohee said this once, without looking up from the pan.

Dojin said yellow.

She prodded the yolk with the tip of a spatula. It resisted, intact. The white had gone opaque at the edges. The yolk sat in the center of that whiteness and it was the color it was.

"Maybe the light in here," he said.

She didn't answer. She slid the egg onto the plate and set it in front of him and poured her coffee and stood at the window. The strip of ocean between the terminal building and the breakwater had the same flat aluminum quality it had in October. She blinked. The orange moved out of her eyes slowly, like a thumbprint wiped from a lens.

He ate. She heard the fork touch the plate.

The apartment light was yellow or white depending on who you asked. The first winter, the wall color — she said blue-gray, he said gray — and they had stood in the same room looking at the same wall. The wall was two colors. The room held both. They moved their furniture into it anyway.

She put her hands on the counter, fingers spread. The stone was cool. She pressed down and felt nothing diagnostic, just the flatness of the surface meeting the flatness of her attention.

He said the egg was good.

She said mm.

After breakfast they walked to the breakwater the way they had walked to the breakwater for years of Saturdays, the walk and the water and the particular quality of silence between them, which had always been comfortable until this morning, when it was something she was monitoring rather than inhabiting.

The railing was cold. She put her hands on it, palms down, and looked out at the water. The ship was four kilometers out. A bulk carrier. It sat wrong — no drift, no response to the swell, as if placed there and forgotten. Dark along the body, red at the waterline. She looked at it for a long time before she said anything.

She said: There.

Dojin turned. His eyes went to her face first. She caught this. Then his gaze tracked out along her pointing arm to the horizon. The pause before he spoke was longer than the egg pause.

He said the haze was strange today.

She took her phone out and photographed it. Zoomed. Something was there — a mass against the horizon, dark, low, the geometry of a loaded carrier. Or of haze shaped by expectation. She looked for the red at the waterline, the detail that would settle it, and found what might have been a red band or might have been the place where the pale sky met the water and picked up color from below. She looked at Dojin. He was looking at the same horizon. His expression was the expression of a person looking at water: neutral, receptive, slightly emptied out. Not a person choosing not to mention a ship. A person who sees water.

She put the phone away and looked again with her bare eyes.

The ship sat on the horizon. Red along the waterline.

On the walk back to the car, neither of them spoke. She counted the seconds between his steps and her own. They were not synchronized. They had never been synchronized, or had been for a while and then drifted, and she had not noticed the drift until now, when she was counting.

In the car he was quiet for the drive back. She ran her thumb along the seatbelt latch, finding the seam where the plastic had begun to separate. A clean crack, not deep, but present. And it was the crack that brought back Tongyeong — three years ago, August, the channel light at dusk, low, flickering on the surface, the kind of light that moves and then appears to not have moved, the way a second hand sometimes appears to stall. She had said do you see it without specifying what. His yes arrived one beat after the question. His eyes had been on her pointing finger.

She knew this now. She had not known it then. She had filed it somewhere and not looked at it, and now she was looking at it, and behind it were the other ones. The dog at 3 a.m. that only she had heard. The apartment light. The wall. The color of an egg. A hundred small divergences she had absorbed into the ordinary because that is what you do across — you absorb the small ones, you let the smoothed-over surface become the surface you live on. You mistake accommodation for agreement. You mistake his yes for arrival when it was echo.

He drove. He said something about traffic. She said mm and watched the city come back in through the windshield, and in the apartment they separated for two hours by unspoken agreement — she to the desk, he to the couch with a book. She opened her laptop. She did not work. She looked at the photograph on her phone. The shape in the water. Dark. Low. What she was calling a ship.

At 5:30 she went to the kitchen and he came in ten minutes later and they began making dinner with the coordination of two people who have made dinner in the same kitchen for eight years and no longer need to negotiate the space. He took the left burner; she took the right. He reached past her for the colander and she moved two inches without looking up. He salted the water. She heard the pan tick as the metal expanded with the heat.

The neighbor's dog started at 6:15. Three short barks, one sustained — the pattern she had memorized without intending to, the way you memorize the sound of a particular car on your street, a particular door. She registered it. She watched Dojin not register it. His hands continued what they were doing. His face did not change.

She put her palms flat on the counter, fingers spread. The stone was warm now from the stove. She pressed down. He said something about the colleague from the Busan office, a story she had heard partially last week and was now receiving the continuation of, and she said mm at the right intervals and her palms stayed on the counter and she could feel the faint vibration of the boiling water, the small systemic shudder of the stone, and she thought: I am feeling for the frequency. I am standing at the one spot in the kitchen where the strip of water is visible and I am pressing my hands against the counter and I am listening for the dog and I am doing all of this instead of saying the one sentence.

I think I'm seeing things you're not.

Or the one underneath that: I don't know which of us is wrong.

Or the third, which she would not say: I'm afraid it's me. I'm more afraid it isn't.

She did not say any of them. The strip of ocean in the window had gone dark. She did not check for the ship. He did not offer to look.

They ate at the table, the one with the cigarette burn in the corner that neither of them had made and both of them had decided not to ask about — evidence of someone else's evening, imported. She had her chair. He had his. She ate half and pushed the rest around and he noticed and said nothing and she was glad he said nothing because she did not want the solicitude arriving in his voice just after she needed it, the way his yes arrived just after her pointing finger. He always arrived. Just not at the same time.

After dinner she washed and he dried. He set each dish back in its place and she heard the soft placement, the precise fit of objects returned to position. He paused once, his hands on the rim of a bowl, his face turned toward the window. She looked. The breakwater lights were on. Nothing that she could see had changed. He finished drying the bowl and set it on the shelf. Very practiced at standing this close and not touching. His arm crossed hers reaching for the pan. She stepped left.

He said something about tomorrow. She said yes.

The kitchen light was yellow. Or white. She looked at it and chose not to decide.

She went to the window one more time, after he had gone to the other room, and stood at the spot where the strip of water was visible between the terminal and the breakwater. The water was dark. The horizon was the line between two grades of darkness, nothing more. She stood there for a long time, aware that she was waiting for something to either be there or not be there, and that the waiting would not resolve — that tomorrow morning she would stand at this same spot and the ship would sit on the water or it would not, and Dojin would come in and set the burner to medium and she would turn it back down and neither of them would comment on this because neither of them had ever commented on this.

She pressed her palms against the glass. Cold. Slightly damp from the day. Giving nothing back.

She left her handprints there and went to bed.

In the other room he was reading. She could hear the specific sound of his page turns — the slight hesitation before each one, the pause that was not the sentence finishing but him deciding. She had always liked this about him. She still liked it. That was not the problem.

She lay in the dark and listened.

His page turned.
