Andrew's Window

By Anon 1,264 words 7.97/7

Prompt

A man has this dream. He calls about fifteen close friends — from different eras of his life, different origins of friendship — for a getaway. Two days, one night. He says, 'If you have anyone to invite, feel free,' and steps out. When he comes back, the place is buckling with a hundred people he doesn't know. His good friend Andrew invited someone, who invited someone, and it piled up. They had three cars. Now they need a taxi service. His ride never shows up. He doesn't get to drive. He feels like he lost the steering wheel of everything. The place where everyone is gathering — it's his old home. The one his family lost because his father lost all the money. As time passes, the rooms transform into something he doesn't recognize. His backpack is somewhere under the pile of a hundred strangers' things. He tries to find it. Can't. The people feel different from him — more casual, more surface-level. He's a more serious type. These people are not his people. A hundred people need a leader. He asks Andrew: we should choose who makes decisions. Andrew says he already took care of it. We already have people to make decisions. Unfortunately, it's not you. Apologetically. A done thing. He decides to leave. He feels bad because fifteen people came because of what he envisioned. He tries to reach them individually — but some have already left, and there are too many strangers in the way. He loses his clothes. He's half-naked, trying to find something to wear. He needs his shoes to walk out. He can't find them. They're somewhere under the pile of a hundred strangers' coats and bags. He's on his knees looking. This is the center of the dream — not the hundred people, not Andrew's apology, not the rooms transforming. The man who decided to leave and can't find what he needs to walk out. The scene shifts. He's at a cafe in front of a gigantic mansion — someone impossibly rich. He moves inside. The same thing: too many people, his stuff lost, his backpack gone. He gets into a fight with a guy — and he's very good at fighting. He kicks his ass. The guy was annoying and it was related to his plan not going well. Around this time he half-realizes it might be a dream. Still no shoes. The lady of the house — super rich, he doesn't know where she came from — sends servants to help him find them. They can't. He is still looking. NOTE: This is a dream. The story must be FIRST PERSON. The world operates on dream logic — stairs miscount, locations bleed into each other, floors change material, things vanish while you look away. The dreamer doesn't announce it's a dream. The world just behaves this way. The primary absence is: he is no longer the person who gets to organize meaning in this house. Everything else orbits that. Let the writing find what the dreamer hasn't found yet.

The floor knows me. Eleven steps to the bathroom, nine steps to the kitchen, the board near the radiator that dips a quarter inch and gives back a sound like a question mark. My feet navigate. The apartment is two hundred and forty square feet and I can cross it in the dark without touching anything, without thinking, without once deciding where to put my weight.

The notebook is on the counter next to my keys. Graph paper, pocket-sized, spine cracked at an angle specific to the way I hold it — cracked left because I'm right-handed and I press the cover back when I write. The first two notebooks are in a box under the bed I could find if I needed to, which I won't. This one has phone numbers in the back. Hyun-woo's landline. A Seoul area code I don't recognize anymore. I never transferred them. They're fine where they are, and the counter is where I left it, and Andrew's birthday is a reasonable thing to hold in this apartment I know the dimensions of by feel.

Fifteen people is reasonable if you account for the fact that most of them will be in the kitchen and the kitchen opens to the small area I call the living room which opens through a half-wall to what I call the study, and people are compressible, they fold toward each other, they find the refrigerator and stay near it. I set my backpack by the door. I open the windows. Someone brings wine and someone else brings the woman who brought the wine and someone else brings a couple who brought more wine and a woman with a clipboard, and I am still smiling when my backpack migrates from the door to under a coat, which is reasonable, to under several coats, which is also reasonable, one at a time.

I cross the board near the radiator three times bringing glasses from the kitchen. It speaks each time. On the third pass someone has set a plant on the floor against the wall and I step around it and land somewhere different and the board is silent. I keep walking because there are thirty people in the studio apartment. Thirty-five. I stop counting and go back to the door because my backpack is under the coats. I know this because I put it there and I have not moved it. I am reaching through gray wool, someone's scarf, a jacket sleeve with a broken zipper that catches on my knuckle. Nylon. Leather, cold. A mesh side pocket. Another nylon. I find the baseboard with my left hand. Press for the notch I made two years ago marking where the baseboard heaved in winter. Flat drywall. Recently painted.

I lift another jacket. Underneath is a notebook. The wrong size. Lined paper, not graph. Someone else's handwriting in the margins — a phone number, an address in a city I've never been to. I set it down where I found it. I lift the next jacket. A rain poncho folded into its own hood. A hat with a scarf attached. A child's glove detached from nothing.

My knees are on hardwood and then tile and I didn't move.

I count the coats. I lose count. I start again from a different coat and lose count at a different number. In the next room Marcus is laughing with a laugh that means he's performing the laugh and not feeling it, and I stand up and go toward it because I can't keep kneeling here.

There are fifty people in the studio apartment now, or there have always been fifty, or fifteen became fifty the way my backpack migrated from the door to under several coats, one at a time. A woman I don't know is sitting on my desk with her legs crossed, talking to a man I don't know about something they are both certain of, and they are sitting on my desk the way people sit on furniture that belongs to the room and not to anyone in particular. Andrew is standing near the window with his back to me. He is telling someone about a route. He is saying you take the bridge not the tunnel, never the tunnel, the bridge is always faster even when it seems slower. I know this because I called everyone and planned the route. I am standing behind Andrew saying his name.

Andrew says: we already have people to make decisions. Not to me. He is still facing the window. He says: unfortunately it's not you.

I hear myself say: I called everyone. I hear myself say: I know where the fuse box is. There is a woman with a clipboard and I am listing things for her. The route. The fuse box. The board near the radiator and which boards around it speak at which pitches. I say: I have been here three years. I say: the first two notebooks are in a box under the bed. The fifteen are standing with their backs partly turned. Andrew has not turned around. I go to the door to get my shoes.

The pile is still there. The coats have compacted, the bottom layer invisible. I pull the top coat off. The next is heavier. The next is a sleeping bag that has partially unrolled and I fold it back and push it to the wall. Under that is a rain jacket. Under that is a rain jacket. Under that is a pair of shoes that could be mine. I pick one up. It fits. It is not mine. I set it down. My notebook is not here. There are a hundred people's things on the same square footage of floor and everything has equal weight.

The hallway is seventeen steps. I counted once on my way back from the door and came up with seventeen instead of eleven. I counted again. Seventeen. The board near the radiator is silent when my feet find it. Someone sistered it from underneath. Reinforced the joist, stopped the give. I press and get nothing back.

I am in the hallway with no shoes. The floor is hardwood under my left foot and tile under my right. I didn't cross a threshold. I look down. Both feet are on hardwood. The room behind me is full, a hum the apartment never made when I lived here alone, a hum it has learned from fifty conversations it is holding all at once.

Someone has moved the plant from the floor to the counter where the notebook was. The plant is a succulent in a white ceramic pot that I don't own. The counter is where I left it. I hadn't said: I put it there when I moved in and it hasn't moved since. I hadn't said: I'm on the third one. I hadn't said any of this to the woman with the clipboard because she has a clipboard and clipboards are for information she already has.

Andrew says my name. He has turned around. This is the first time. He says: you're still here. He turns back to the window.

Anyway, I say.

Nothing follows it. The word stands in the doorway. My feet are bare and the floor is a material I don't recognize. Not hardwood, not tile. Something that gives slightly, like I'm standing on the floor someone put over the floor. I don't look down. I am still in the room. The board near the radiator does not speak. I press my foot into the floor and listen.
