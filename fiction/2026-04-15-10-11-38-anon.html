The Guide Shoe

By Anon 1,431 words 8.47/7

The A-car has a guide shoe wearing unevenly on the left rail. Delphine knows this the way she knows most things: she heard it first, three mornings ago, a lateral shimmy at the third-floor approach, and then she listened for it again the next morning to be sure, and then she ordered the insert, and now she has it in her kit. Diagnosis, procurement, intervention. She opens the access panel at 22:47.

The hospital at night holds itself differently. The fluorescents stay the same temperature, the same lux, but fewer bodies interrupt the light and so the light changes. Delphine has been here . She knows which tiles are slightly higher than their neighbors. She knows the east stairwell door on three sings in dry weather — the hinge, the specific dry-weather pitch of it. She knows the insert she is pressing into place now will hold for fourteen months under current load conditions, and she knows this because she has been replacing guide shoe inserts in this building for and the numbers are reliable.

She does not know why Dr. Achour started riding the elevator at 23:40 instead of 23:15.

Twenty-five minutes. Consistent for three weeks. Delphine presses the insert home, checks the seating, replaces the panel. She writes the repair in her logbook. The entry takes four lines. The information about Achour takes zero lines.

She has to ride the car to test it. She presses five. The shimmy is gone. The A-car rises smoothly, the guide shoe tracking clean on the left rail, and Delphine stands with her eyes closed and her hands slightly open and she feels the car through the soles of her shoes — a practice she has never described to anyone. She feels the elevator.

Fifth floor. The landing door opens onto the oncology corridor. This door has been running slightly out of plumb, a two-millimeter variance she logged last Tuesday. She takes out her level. She checks the door frame. The variance is the same: two millimeters, within tolerance but worth monitoring.

At 23:41, Dr. Achour comes out of the stairwell.

Delphine does not look up from her level. She registers: rubber-soled shoes with a worn right heel. Dark trousers, pressed. A gait that is deliberate but not urgent — the deliberateness of someone who has decided not to hurry, which is different from the deliberateness of someone who has never hurried. He walks past the elevator and stops at the end of the corridor, at a window she had not factored into his route, and he stands there.

She watches the bubble.

He stands at the window for two minutes and four seconds. She knows this because she counts — not intentionally, the way she counts most things, the number simply accruing. The window faces east, toward Fourvière. At this hour, from this floor, on a clear night, the basilica lit against the hill. Delphine has never stood at that window. The machine room has no windows.

Achour enters the ward. Delphine logs the door plumb. She notes that the variance has not changed. On the ride down, she runs the weight-sensor data she has accumulated without intending to accumulate it: Achour, November readings versus current. Four kilograms. She had not calculated this before she thought it just now, which means she had the inputs stored somewhere she was not supervising. She presses B.

The landing door seal on five also has a gap of approximately three millimeters on the east side, enough to let cold air in from the shaft. Delphine discovers this on a Wednesday. She has her caulking gun, her cleaning rag, the appropriate sealant. She closes the gap in twenty minutes. In her logbook: Fifth-floor landing door east seal replaced. Draft eliminated. Comfort of occupants.

She chose Wednesday because Achour is not on the ward Wednesday nights. He will find the draft gone on Thursday and have no mechanism for connecting its absence to her. The repair was needed. She made the repair.

She is writing the logbook entry when she sees Bertrand's handwriting above hers, the green ink he uses, the specific way he forms his sevens with the little crossbar, and she realizes she could identify his handwriting at a distance, in dim light, before reading a single digit. She does not know what he looks like in good light. Her own writing in the logbook is slower tonight, more careful.

The B-car's counterweight cable tension has been running three percent high for . Not actionable yet, but worth noting, and so she notes it, and somewhere in the noting the sentence accumulates: cable tension B-car port-side high three percent, monitor weekly, check against Favreau's Tuesday pattern, he rides alone to four, the weight reads ninety-one kilograms, and three months ago it read eighty-nine, and the A-car landing two floors below Achour's window shows a load of seventy-eight kilograms at 23:40 on nights when the corridor light is on because someone is in there before the elevator arrives, and the sixth floor at 03:15 carries a smell she has categorized as: salt, exhaustion, something that is not quite grief but is the physical fact of grief before language organizes it, and the elevator holds this smell for approximately four minutes before the ventilation moves it, and Delphine has ridden through that smell on twelve separate occasions and on none of those occasions has she logged it.

She stops writing.

She reads the sentence back.

She tears the page out.

The logbook is property of the hospital. She is removing a page from hospital property. She folds the page once, twice, and puts it in the chest pocket of her coverall. She can feel its edge against her sternum through the fabric. Cable tension B-car port-side high three percent, monitor weekly. That is the entry. The page in her pocket is also what happened.

At 04:30, she replaces the carbon brushes in the A-car motor. The brushes wear at a predictable rate and she replaces them on a predictable schedule. While she works she thinks about the building: where each car is, which floors are being requested, the rhythm of the overnight calls. Four has been busy — the nurses' station, the medication run. Seven has been quiet. Three has a patient who calls the elevator and then does not ride; the weight sensor reports empty on arrival, and this has happened seven times since February. The brushes are seated. She closes the motor housing and turns off the light and picks up her kit.

The corridor at 05:00 runs from the machine room toward the service elevator. She becomes aware of her own footsteps — not to diagnose them, not to categorize them, just listening, the way she listens to the counterweight at the top of its travel, where the motor holds its breath before reversing. She sounds like someone who has been walking through this building for , recording everything, logging nothing, carrying a folded page in her chest pocket against her sternum, whose hands are cold, whose hands are always cold by the second hour.

Achour's footsteps are behind her.

She knows this before she has decided to know it. The wear pattern on the right heel, the left-forward roll, the specific weight of deliberate-but-not-urgent — she has it indexed, cross-referenced, available.

The corridor is empty ahead. The service elevator is thirty meters away. The fluorescents stay the same temperature, the same lux, and Delphine is standing in it, stock-still, her kit in her right hand, listening to the absence of sound behind her the way she listens to the motor pause, the held breath, the moment before the system decides which direction to move.

She has not turned. She has not kept walking. She is in the pause.

In her chest pocket, folded twice, against her sternum: a page about a window facing east, about a weight differential, about a smell she has ridden through twelve times, about a draft she closed on a Wednesday, about the long sentence she knows she will write again, differently, because she cannot stop knowing, and the machine is her, and she is standing in a hospital corridor at five in the morning with her hands cold and her handwriting careful and her logbook two pages lighter than it should be, and Achour is five meters behind her in rubber-soled shoes.

She does not turn. She waits one beat, then two, the pause at the top of travel, the motor holding, the whole counterweight system suspended in the moment before it becomes motion again.

She keeps walking.
