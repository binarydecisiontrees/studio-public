The Bread Basket

By Anon 1,542 words 8.26/7

Three old friends meet for dinner. One of them is about to ask the other two for money. None of them knows which one it is.

The bread basket arrives before anyone has unfolded a napkin.

Yanwei takes the wine list first. Not because he wants wine — he wants the shield of looking at something other than Soo-jin and David while his chest does what his chest has been doing for three weeks. The list is burgundy leather, pressed to mimic something older. He holds it and thinks about his own menus, the ones with his name on the back inside a small oval, and then he puts the thought away because he's been putting that thought away every twenty minutes for three weeks.

Soo-jin's sparkling water is already sweating. She ordered it before the hugs even, because she needed something in her hands. David is still at the coat check. He walks back the way he always walks in a restaurant — shoulders forward, body still conducting the conversation from the street — and when he sees her watching he spreads his arms. They hug for four seconds. One second too long. The second when his mouth was near her ear and she could have said David, listen, and didn't.

"Where's Yanwei?"

"Wine list. Spiritual."

The laugh is real.

Yanwei hears it and feels something loosen in his sternum. He puts down the wine list and stands and they do the thing they do — the hug that turns into David gripping his shoulders and looking at him, actually looking, the way that used to feel intrusive and now feels like being seen. A half-second where he could say it. Three words. His diaphragm contracts for it. David says you look good, man, you actually look good and the window closes.

They sit. Sourdough and something seeded, the crust the right color. Soo-jin tears a piece and holds it while the sommelier materializes — the specific quality of someone briefed on their own warmth. Yanwei says something about a producer from the Rhône, a small name, a specific vintage. The sommelier's expression does a thing — not dismissal, just the half-beat of someone reaching for a recognition they won't find — and Yanwei catches it and Soo-jin catches Yanwei catching it. An excellent choice. Gone.

He used to know every sommelier in this part of the city. Six years of dinners, of tabs, of being the restaurateur who understood the arithmetic of a room from both sides. Three months shuttered. Already becoming someone who gets the half-beat.

He reaches for the bread at the same moment David does. David tears his piece too hard, always too hard, eating while watching something across the room. Soo-jin watches his jaw. He has no idea. Three versions rehearsed on the walk over. Maybe tonight is just tonight. But the grant office has not been wrong before. Forty-three thousand dollars. Plus the year of preliminary work.

She breaks more bread.

"Marcus and Priya," David says.

"I know."

"I saw them last week. Marcus is— he seems okay. More okay than you'd expect."

"He always seemed okay," Yanwei says. "That was the problem."

The conversation finds a current. Marcus and Priya. David's daughter's apartment, painted entirely yellow. The old building on Burnside, Yanwei's first location, the landlord with vintage motorcycles who kept one in the stairwell and told everyone it was a space installation. The bread is good. She eats more.

For four minutes she is not performing anything.

The menus arrive and hers lies flat. She reads it as a legal document. Prices printed small, as if the number's humiliation decreases with font size. Pork belly, $34. She closes the menu.

David holds his at arm's length. Not because he needs glasses — he keeps forgetting to pick up new glasses. He does not look at prices. Hasn't in seven years. He orders the short rib.

Under the table, Soo-jin's hand closes on her napkin. The short rib is sixty-two dollars. Her account has $1,147. Lease co-signed on a lab, eleven months remaining. Split evenly: move something from savings. She's been moving things from savings since February. She orders the trout. Twenty-six dollars. She has rehearsed the trout.

David's phone buzzes against his thigh and he flips it before anyone can read the screen. Too fast. Yanwei reads it as work. Soo-jin reads it as rudeness. Both wrong. Third call today from the same 212 number. He doesn't pick up because picking up is a different kind of becoming someone. At this table he is still the person who knows wine regions, coaches lacrosse on Saturdays, has opinions about the bread.

"Sorry."

"Don't be."

The waiter, passing, asks if anyone is in the industry. He asks because of the Rhône producer, the easy familiarity. Yanwei opens his mouth. Closes it.

"Adjacent," David says.

Yanwei picks up his wine glass. Soo-jin watches him drink. He's fine. The Meyers catering contracts. His wife said so in December. In December Yanwei's wife had not yet been told about the books. She knows now. Soo-jin doesn't know she knows. The Meyers certainty is a story Soo-jin tells herself with conviction, and no one is going to correct it tonight.

By the time the appetizers come they've been to Portugal — two years discussed, never booked — the coastal town Yanwei recommended, Soo-jin's mother moving in with her sister in Busan, which is fine, which is actually fine, no really, the way you say really when something is mostly fine. The wine is good. The light is warm. Ten years of practice at conversation that carries itself. And beneath it Soo-jin is revising. I have something I need to tell you both. No. Things have gotten complicated with the grant. No. Can I— She feels it in her throat. Can I— And then forty-three thousand by end of quarter or fold the lab. She kept David's apartment during the dissolution. Found Yanwei's contractor. She is the person you call. To become the person who calls—

The look on their faces. She can't get past it.

David refills her glass. Forty thousand by Friday or something gets liquidated his wife doesn't know is margined. Short rib, mistake, sixty-two. Wine by the glass, eighteen. Split evenly, eighty-seven with tax. Account: forty-two hundred. Tonight is fine. Friday isn't. The pour is steady.

Across from him Yanwei is somewhere else — hearing his restaurant at full capacity. Not the din but the specific music. A Tuesday at ten PM, different frequency than Saturday at eight, fewer voices but more comfortable ones, the regulars who sent a glass to his table and he would raise it across the room. He surfaces. Soo-jin is looking at him.

"Sorry. I'm here."

"You're allowed to be elsewhere."

Something in her face. Not pity. Close to recognition. He files it. Immediately unfiles it — she has the lab, the grants, the university structure. She has always had the structure.

"Can I—" Soo-jin says.

"Listen, I—" David says.

They stop. The laugh comes, both of them, the real kind, before you decide to laugh, and Yanwei laughs too, and in that laughter is the place where all three asks almost touched, almost, a place that had geography for half a second, and then the entrées arrive and it closes.

"Remember the garlic bread," Yanwei says. "The Burnside place."

"The entire loaf. Every time."

"Every time. Without asking."

"We would get there and it would already be there. On the table. Like it lived there."

The entrées are good. Someone mentions a bar that closed. A bar that should have closed. The wine is almost gone. Yanwei refills without checking the bottle, and then the check is there, in its leather fold, without anyone having asked.

All three hands move at once.

Yanwei reaches with the same ease in his face, the same gesture, everything the same except the account, which his wife checks Saturdays, which has $847, which is enough tonight and sixty dollars more than he can spend.

"My treat."

David pulls back. Soo-jin doesn't pull back all the way. Yanwei takes it.

$312, with tax.

Card down. No hesitation. The waiter takes it.

David watches the card go.

"You don't have to—"

"I want to."

She looks at his face. Something moves in her chest. She almost says it. Are you— The Meyers event. December. The recognition finds an explanation and settles. She lets him pay.

The signature: fast, not looking. The muscle memory of someone who signed twenty checks a night. The signature looks like someone who is fine.

Outside, cold at once, breath visible, three clouds. Soo-jin's coat buttoned wrong — one off from the top. She starts to speak. Actually, there's something— The cold enters her mouth.

David puts his hand on Yanwei's shoulder and squeezes once. Yanwei covers it with his own hand, two seconds. Apart.

"Let's do this again soon."

"Yes. Definitely."

"Definitely."

Three different parking structures. Soo-jin's heels on concrete. The IRS letter in Yanwei's glovebox, second notice, amount due in bold. David's equity gone. The grant rejection in Soo-jin's phone, swinging against her hip. Footsteps. One person's footsteps. No footsteps.

Through frosted glass — the table they left. Three water glasses. A crumpled napkin. The bread basket, two pieces, bottom still warm.

It looks like a table where something good happened.

It looks exactly like that.
