The Fourth Chair

By Anon 1,559 words 8.47/7

Prompt

A woman stands in her father's kitchen holding his phone face-down on the table, waiting for her brother to arrive — an old family secret and a new one colliding

She pours the coffee. Two mugs. His with more sugar than a grown man should need.

He sits with his hands around the mug the way he always has, thumbs touching at the top, and says the gutters are going to be a problem come fall. She says is that right. He says the ice gets in the seam and once that happens you're not patching you're replacing, and he sets the mug down and wipes the counter with the heel of his palm, that slow arc, bearing down slightly. She watches it. She has watched it her whole life and called it something else.

The phone is face-down between them.

She lifts her mug and smells citrus. His soap on her hands, the wrong brand, the one she bought because he was visiting. She put it out like a setting she'd arranged. The kitchen is full of things she arranged. He says he could look at the gutters after they eat, if she wants a second opinion before she calls someone. She says that would be helpful. She pours herself coffee she doesn't want because not pouring it would be a tell. The fourth chair is two inches from the table. She has been measuring things with her eye for .

She reaches for the sugar and she is nine years old in the back seat of a car and her mother is saying he won't make it, the roads are closed, the governor said, and her mother's voice has that particular pitch, the one that means she is already moving past disappointment into a story she can tell at Thanksgiving, and then the headlights come through the window and her mother goes quiet and there he is, her father, standing in the door with frost in his eyebrows, white, and her mother says nine hours in this and he says there are some things you just go to and she remembers thinking: this is what love looks like. The sugar spoon is heavier than she remembered.

She stirs her coffee. She says so what about the seam, is there something she can do in the meantime. He says you can put tape on it, honestly, it's not elegant but tape in the right place holds through one winter if you press it properly. She nods. She says she'll do that. She will not think about the tape on his phone. She will not think about it here, in this kitchen, with his hands around her mug. He says how's work. She says fine, busy, the Hendricks project is almost done. He says Hendricks, that's the — she says the commercial one, yes. He says ah. He wipes the counter again though there's nothing on it. She watches his hands and thinks: four minutes. Forty-seven Thursdays. Someone who calls for four minutes has a window they're managing. She works in architecture.

The clock between the fridge and the pantry makes no sound. She keeps hearing it. He says he should have called before coming, he knew she had the Hendricks thing, he says you always have something and he says it with pride, not complaint, and she looks at his face and it is warm. The warmth is in his eyes and the set of his jaw and the way he leans slightly forward when she talks, the way he always has.

The door opens. Darius drops his keys on the counter on the cleaned surface. He smells like outside air and car heat and the kitchen, which had been sealed, has weather in it now. Her father's face does something — a warmth, a realignment — and she catches it because she is watching for it, and she cannot tell if she caught a performance or a genuine thing, and she looks at her hands. Still smelling of citrus. Her father says Darius you want coffee. Darius says obviously. Her father reaches past her, past the phone, takes the mug with the chipped handle, the one Darius cracked in college and they never replaced. He fills it without measuring the sugar. He knows the amount.

Darius sits. He puts his elbows on the table in the way their mother spent fifteen years discouraging. He looks at her father and says so gutters. He says it like he already knew. Her father says come fall, it's going to be a thing, and Darius says you say that every year and her father says and every year I'm right. Darius laughs. She laughs. The laugh is real. She reaches for the creamer and she is in the kitchen of the house on Meridian Street, thirteen, and her mother is on the phone with someone, and her mother's voice has a quality she will not identify for twenty years, a particular brightness, an alertness, and her father comes in from the garage and her mother says I have to go, it's Robert and hangs up, and her father says who was that and her mother says Janet, and her father says ah and goes to the sink. She stood in that doorway for a long time.

The creamer is in her hand. She pours it. She sets it down. Darius is saying something about his daughter's school play, he says she got the lead and then says something about it being the understudy but she's still thrilled, and their father says she gets that from her mother and he means Darius's partner, he means Camille, and Nadia waits for something to move in his face. Nothing moves. But Darius's face goes still. The stillness of someone who has practiced. She is wearing it too, and she looks across the table at her brother. She has been making it for . The math of it, clean at 3 AM, the phone bill spread across the bathroom floor, the name she didn't recognize and then recognized. Who calls for four minutes. Someone with a window.

She looks at the phone. Darius reaches for the sugar. He says, without looking up, he calls on Thursdays, you know. Not to her. To the sugar bowl. To the air in front of him.

She had a whole architecture. of it. The research, the reclassifications, the precision she kept like a wound she'd dressed herself, and the architecture drops through the floor in the time it takes Darius to set the sugar spoon down. He knows. He has known. She looks at her father, who is looking at his mug, thumbs touching at the top, and his face is doing nothing, and she understands that he heard and is letting it be heard and is not going to speak.

Her father says anyone hungry, and he gets up and opens the fridge. Darius says sure. She says sure. Her father pulls out containers, the leftovers from Sunday, and he sets them on the counter and wipes the counter first, that long arc, the heel of the palm bearing down, and she watches him do it. She watches him arrange the containers in a row. She watches him pull the plates from the cabinet — three of them — and set them on the cleaned surface. They are the plates she grew up with, the ones her father gave her when she moved and said keep them, I have more. She has more now. So does he. He opens the microwave and loads the first container. The microwave hums its sixty-second note. The phone is still face-down on the table. No one has touched it. Darius is looking at his coffee. Her father is watching the microwave like it requires supervision. There is a version of this moment in which she picks up the phone and puts it on the table face-up and says there's a name in here I need you to explain and there is a version of this moment in which she does not. She does not.

The microwave stops. Her father takes out the container, replaces it with the next, presses the buttons in the sequence she has watched him press her entire life. He calls on Thursdays. He drove nine hours in a storm and let frost get into his eyebrows. The eyebrows were real. The driving was real. The thing that made him drive might have also been real. She keeps trying to find the place where the love ends and the performance begins and she cannot find it. The fourth chair. He pulls it back in as he walks past it, his hip, the casual hip-check of a man in his own kitchen, and the chair slides flush against the table. She watches it happen. The same way she corrected the soap. Darius says something about the play again, something about the costumes, and their father laughs, that laugh, the one she has known since before she knew what genuine meant.

He sets the third plate on the table, in front of her, and says eat something, the same thing he said every Thursday morning through the whole of her childhood, and she looks at the plate, her mother's plate, her plate, the plate he carried in a box down the stairs when she moved out, and she picks up her fork. The food is warm. He warmed it. He knew the amounts. The phone stays face-down on the table.
