The Pile

By Anon 1,162 words 7.67/7 ★★★★☆

He calls about fifteen close friends — from different eras of his life, different origins of friendship — for a getaway. Two days, one night. He says, 'If you have anyone to invite, feel free,' and steps out. When he comes back, the place is buckling with a hundred people he doesn't know. His good friend Andrew invited someone, who invited someone, and it piled up. They had three cars. Now they need a taxi service. His ride never shows up. He doesn't get to drive. He feels like he lost the steering wheel of everything.

The place where everyone is gathering — it's his old home. The one his family lost because his father lost all the money. As time passes, the rooms transform into something he doesn't recognize. His backpack is somewhere under the pile of a hundred strangers' things. He tries to find it. Can't. The people feel different from him — more casual, more surface-level. He's a more serious type. These people are not his people.

A hundred people need a leader. He asks Andrew: we should choose who makes decisions. Andrew says he already took care of it. 'We already have people to make decisions. Unfortunately, it's not you.' Apologetically. A done thing.

He decides to leave. He feels bad because fifteen people came because of what he envisioned. He tries to reach them individually — but some have already left, and there are too many strangers in the way. He loses his clothes. He's half-naked, trying to find something to wear. He needs his shoes to walk out. He can't find them. They're somewhere under the pile of a hundred strangers' coats and bags. He's on his knees looking.

This is the center of the dream — not the hundred people, not Andrew's apology, not the rooms transforming. The man who decided to leave and can't find what he needs to walk out.

The scene shifts. He's at a café in front of a gigantic mansion — someone impossibly rich. He moves inside. The same thing: too many people, his stuff lost, his backpack gone. He gets into a fight with a guy — and he's very good at fighting. He kicks his ass. The guy was annoying and it was related to his plan not going well. Around this time he half-realizes it might be a dream. Still no shoes. The lady of the house — super rich, he doesn't know where she came from — sends servants to help him find them. They can't. He is still looking.

The shoes are under the coats. I know this because I put them there. The party is loud in the way that means I've been here too long — not the good loud, the loud where individual voices become a weather system. I can feel it in my teeth.

My left sock is wet. I was in the kitchen. Then I was here.

I need the shoes. The white ones, the ones with the crushed left heel because I never untie them, I just step in and flatten the back and Andrew told me once that's why my knees hurt and I said it's not and he said it is and I still do it. They're the only pair I brought. One pair of shoes to a place with gravel outside and no sidewalks. If I can't find them I can't leave. The gravel goes all the way to the road.

Everyone is in the other room. No one else needs to leave. I am the only person in this building who is trying to go somewhere. The going requires the shoes and the shoes are under the pile and the pile keeps being replenished. I clear a layer and hear a coat drop behind me. Soft thud. Nylon on nylon. My hands smell like other people's pockets. Lint and receipt paper and something metallic. I'm sorting fast but the sorting doesn't accumulate. Each jacket I move is replaced by a jacket I didn't move.

I make a system. Left wall to right. Everything checked goes behind me. Canvas, navy, laces knotted wrong. Nylon, black, a thousand pockets. Leather, cold. A wool coat sleeve. A windbreaker that is wet for the same reason my sock is wet. None of them are mine. I know by touch. The pile does not get smaller. The wool coat is there again. Or still there.

I try to text Andrew. My phone is in the backpack. The backpack is somewhere in the pile. I can see myself arriving — the gravel, the bright yellow door — but the moment of putting the backpack down is not there. Before it and after it. The moment itself is gone. But Andrew's laugh comes from the living room. Short, surprised. I follow it.

The living room has about a hundred people in it. I keep losing the count at forty. Andrew is near the window. I say: is this your party now or mine. His face goes careful. He says, I think unfortunately it's not you anymore. I knew it on the way to asking. But knowing it and hearing it are different and I'm standing there in wet socks not saying anything for a second too long. I want to tell him about the shoes. But Andrew would say just go barefoot. And I would have to explain that the gravel is real and he's never walked on it and I'd be explaining instead of leaving and I'm already not leaving.

There's a window behind him that wasn't there before. Through it I can see an oak tree. I look back at Andrew and he's saying something and I'm watching his mouth and I'm also watching the window and somewhere in the watching my feet are bare now and I'm asking a woman near the hallway, have you seen a black backpack. She hasn't. I asked whether she'd seen it and not where it is and those are different questions. A man near the kitchen says there's a pile of bags near the back. The pile near the back is the same pile. It is in a different room.

Then I see Marcus across the room — Marcus who I called two weeks ago, Marcus who drove four hours for this — and he's laughing at something a stranger said and his laugh is easy in a way that means he's already somewhere I'm not and I look away before he sees me looking.

I'm pulling a coat from the pile and the coat is in the café now. Plastic table. The kind with fold-out legs. The one from the old patio — my father's hands on the edge of it, the crack running through the center that he used to trace with his thumb — I look away from the thought. The ceiling is higher. The crowd is different people but there are the same number of them. Someone near the back is saying something about the plan not working and then a man steps too close and my left foot plants and my right hip rotates and my leg comes up and through and the kick is exact. Every joint reports. His legs go out from under him. He goes down complete. Everything around me stops for half a second. I am perfectly legible in this moment. I am a thing that just happened in a room and the room knows it.

Then I'm on my knees in front of another pile. Tile is cold. White leather shoes near the left side. I reach for them. The heels sit flat and pristine because whoever owns them unties them like Andrew does and sets them down properly and doesn't destroy the backs the way I destroy mine. I put the wrong shoe down. A woman to my left — this is her house — says something to someone behind her and four people spread out and start going through bags and piles in different parts of the room. They are methodical. They talk quietly to each other. One of them brings me a shoe. White and leather. I see the heel. The heel is intact. I turn it over in my hands. I set it down. They come back one by one. Nothing. They don't say sorry. They just come back and stand and the woman looks at me and I look at the pile and neither of us says anything.

The room is quieter now. I can hear the party somewhere but it feels farther away than the walls explain. A tote bag. A windbreaker. A scarf that someone folded before they put it down. I fold the scarf again and put it to the left. Left to right. Maintaining the system. Nylon. Canvas. Something smooth and synthetic with no pockets. A heavy parka that takes both hands. None of them are mine. I know by touch. The light is different. The pile has the same number of objects it always did. There is always more of it below. The pile has a bottom and my hands have not found it yet. My bare feet are cold on the tile. My fingers close on something. I pull it toward me. Fabric. The right color. The texture is wrong but I keep pulling, both hands, the pile shifting and resettling around my wrists, the weight of it holding everything down, the room still going somewhere behind me, and I am still here, still in it, still pulling.
