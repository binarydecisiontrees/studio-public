Andrew's Radiator

By Anon 960 words 7.67/7

Prompt

A man has this dream. He calls about fifteen close friends for a getaway. When he comes back, the place is buckling with a hundred people he doesn't know. His friend Andrew tells him they already chose people to make decisions. Unfortunately, it's not him. He decides to leave but can't find his shoes under a pile of strangers' coats. NOTE: This is a dream. First person. The world operates on dream logic. Never say the word dream.

The ice is in two bags in the trunk because one bag sweats through. I bought the cooler bag last November and it doesn't seal right on the left side so I double-bag and put the weak side up. The ice will hold for forty minutes in this heat, which is enough because the drive is eleven.

The back door latch sticks if you pull straight. You have to lift and pull, lift then pull. I'll stand there.

Fifteen people. I've arranged the chairs offset, not in a circle.

The mug is on the windowsill. I checked. Handle left. It catches light around seven-forty which is when people will be arriving.

The radiator. Left for hot. I will walk every person past it.

I'm in the car. The ice is fine. The list is: ice, the good knife from my apartment because the house knives are dull, a speaker cable because the Bluetooth drops in that room, and lemons. I have the list in my head, not on paper.

. I pull into the drive and the hallway has a coat rack I didn't buy. Chrome standing rack, four hooks, a bar across the bottom for umbrellas, standing in the corner where I keep the emergency candles. Three pairs of shoes I don't recognize. Then four. Then seven. I stop counting shoes.

Someone has connected a speaker. I step past a bag against the wall. I step past a jacket on the floor. There is a cedar smell. The mug is on the sill but it is three inches wrong, handle right. The air is warm. The doorknobs are warm. I touch one to check. The door to the back room is closed and there are voices behind it. I set the ice on the counter and go to find somewhere to put the knife.

There are coats on the chair by the hallway. A heap of them — four, five. I move around them. I set the knife in the drawer and it fits wrong because something in the drawer has shifted. I rearrange two things and the knife fits.

I hear Andrew in the main room explaining the radiator knob. Left for hot, he is saying. Left for hot and don't touch the valve at the bottom. I showed him this. I go back to the hallway.

The coats have moved. Or there are more of them. The heap on the chair has become a drift across the arm and onto the table. I reach into the pile and lift and pull. Nylon. I set it aside. Leather, cold. I set it aside. A mesh side pocket, a strap, canvas. I set it aside. I reach deeper and pull a wool sleeve and underneath it is another wool sleeve. I fold both and set them on the table. I reach again.

Andrew is beside me. He says they took a vote, back in February, and the group grew, it went from fifteen to forty in six weeks, which is a good thing, a healthy thing, and they needed a different kind of space, a different kind of structure, and the collective is the structure now, the collective decides, and everyone got a say. He says I was on the original list, technically. I say: the shoe. He says: what?

I reach past him and lift a down jacket and underneath it is another down jacket and underneath that is a canvas tote. I set the tote on the table. I pull a parka, green, waterproof, and fold it and place it on the chair. I reach again. Nylon. Corduroy. A rubber raincoat that smells like cedar. I fold it and set it on the pile.

The ice in the glasses is clicking from every room. The desk is gone from the east wall. A door I used to walk through without touching the frame is closed now, voices behind it. I circle back to the hallway and the drift of coats has spread from the table to the corner of the room. I go to the corner and lift and check and set aside. Lift and check and set aside.

There is a sign on the bathroom door with a name on it. I go back to the corner. My thumbs are running across the seams of other people's pockets. A phone charger. A folded receipt. An empty pocket — I move my thumb across the inside seam and feel where the lining is starting to separate. I set this one aside. I reach for the next one and find the jacket with the cracked right pocket from when it went down the stairs in January. I pull it from under fifteen others. It smells like cedar now. I hold it and set it on the table with the others.

I look for any surface I placed something on. The thermostat number. The speaker cable I ran behind the baseboard. The chair I angled by the window so the light would come over the left shoulder. The chair is somewhere behind the coats.

I go back to the pile. I lift and check and fold and set aside. Lift and check. Lift and check. The heel appears — brown, pointing up — from somewhere near the middle. I reach for it. I close fingers around the toe of a boot that is not a shoe. I set it down. I reach past it. Nylon. Wool, heavy. I pull. Underneath it is another coat. Underneath that is another coat. Somewhere in the next room Andrew is explaining the radiator, left for hot, and the ice clicks, and I lift and check and fold and set aside, lift and check, lift and check, and the pile does not go down.
