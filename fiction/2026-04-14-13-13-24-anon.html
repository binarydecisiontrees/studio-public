The Recipe Box

By Anon 1,208 words 8.17/7

Prompt

A woman discovers her dead grandmother's recipe box contains not recipes but short, brutal assessments of every person the grandmother ever cooked for. The cards are in her handwriting, filed alphabetically. Some people got multiple entries across decades. The woman finds her own name.

The drawer sticks. Elena's palm angles before she reaches — lift, pull — and it opens on the first try. She is not thinking about the trick of it. She is thinking about the dumpster on Brighton Beach Avenue, whether forty yards is enough, whether the Salvation Army takes pots with burnt handles, whether she will hit bridge traffic.

The drawer holds kitchen twine, a rubber band gone brittle, a wine key from some promotion, a box.

She sees the box and thinks: recipes.

The first card: borscht. Her grandmother's handwriting in blue ballpoint, the early kind, loops closed tight as fists. Ingredients in two columns. At the bottom, the measurement for dill written as enough — not a teaspoon, not a bunch, just enough. She smells it now, the dill, a ghost of it in the wood of the drawer. She turns the card over.

Uncle Grisha: remembers everything, uses nothing. The kind of man who is always right after.

She reads it again. The pen pressure makes the letters lean forward, walking somewhere. She turns it face up — enough — then face down. Always right after. She puts it on the counter and reaches for the next card.

Vera Blum, 3B: keeps her kitchen clean the way certain women keep their opinions — for company, not for use.

Elena almost laughs. Catches it. Lets it go through her nose. She is on her grandmother's side of the knife. Next card:

Ray: good hands, weak follow-through. Makes pie crust better than decisions.

She doesn't know who Ray is. She reaches for the next card before she has finished reading this one.

Tamara V.: funny in the way that makes you look around for who she's laughing at. Found out it was me. Invited her back anyway. You have to eat with someone.

She stops. The turn at the end arrives exactly where she could see it coming and she didn't dodge — you have to eat with someone. Her grandmother was not without loneliness. First time she has considered that as a real fact.

She is reaching for the next card before she has finished understanding this. She is eating them. Reaching for the next one before she has finished chewing the one before, each one sharper, the handwriting looser, ballpoint giving way to felt-tip, and she thinks she was doing this for years — and stops. Looks at the stack remaining. Everyone she fed was also being filed. The stove was a blind. The table was a desk. She looks at the counter where her grandmother stood on a folded towel to reach the back burner and then looks away.

She tests the next card between thumb and forefinger. The stock is thinner. She has crossed into a different decade without noticing, and her mother's card arrives with her full name: Nadia Morozova, née Blum.

The card is not cruel.

That is the first thing Elena registers. Then she reads what it is instead. Three sentences that describe her mother the way you'd describe a winter that lasted too long: precisely, without blame, without warmth, without the implication that anyone is responsible for the weather. The first sentence names a pattern. The second gives an example. The third says still — as in, the observation is active, ongoing, running parallel to the Wednesday dinners and the phone calls and the thirty years of sitting across the kitchen table.

Elena's hands go .

Palms down.

The refrigerator compressor cycles on. She almost speaks — not to anyone, just into the apartment, a sound — and catches it. Almost speaks again and doesn't. She is still on her grandmother's side of the knife. The same clarity that was thrilling in strangers is something else applied to her mother. Not discomfort with the unfairness. Discomfort with the accuracy. Her mother, described in three sentences. Weather.

She puts the card face-down. Picks it up. Reads it again. Puts it face-down. Keeps going.

The thumb-and-forefinger test has become automatic — thin, thick, thin. She is not reading for pleasure anymore. She is reading toward a letter. She can feel the distance to her own name in the width of paper between her fingers.

Avram L.: God-fearing in the way that lets you feel very comfortable about what you do to your family.

Boris K.: taught me to make the dough. I never told him it needed more salt. Some debts you keep.

She keeps going. She is pressing her right thumbnail into her left index finger, a crescent of pressure she is not aware of. The felt-tip era now, white slick stock, the arthritis visible in the changed grip. She is close.

Dmitri: I don't know what he wanted from me but I gave him what I had.

—

Elena Morozova.

Filed formally. Her grandmother called her Lenochka. Called her Lenochka for forty-one years. Lenochka come taste this. Lenochka sit down. Lenochka you look tired. This card was not written to her.

She reads the card the way you read a phrase in a foreign language you half-know — each word arriving separately before the sentence assembles. The felt-tip, which means late, which means the last years, the Wednesday visits, the farmer's cheese from the good store on Neptune Avenue that her grandmother pretended was not expensive. She reads each word at that speed.

What the card says is not a wound.

She checks again. Not a wound. She looks for the wound the way you probe a tooth with your tongue. No wound. She looks for the benediction — she understood me, something close to that — and there is no benediction either. What there is: an observation. The same instrument applied to her that was applied to Grisha and Vera and Ray and Tamara and her own mother. The same handwriting. The same pressure. The same eyes that watched from behind the stove watching her too, all those Wednesdays, the whole time. Elena was not the one who saw her grandmother clearly. Her grandmother watched everyone with the same eyes. Elena was the one who showed up on Wednesdays. The farmer's cheese was also real.

She smells dill.

From the box, or from her own hands, or from the wood of the table. She stands in it. Then she slides the card back between the D's and the F's, the way her grandmother filed it. The cards close around it without ceremony. She fits the lid on the box. Lifts it into the drawer — heel of palm angled up, the lift-and-pull in reverse — and pushes it in.

The twine goes back. The rubber band is broken, so she leaves it alongside everything else and pushes the drawer closed.

It doesn't stick.

She stands with her hands at her sides. She is still pressing her right thumbnail into her left index finger, a white crescent. She does not notice this. She notices the refrigerator is still humming, the afternoon light has moved across the counter to the corner where the chipped blue bowl sits in a cardboard box with the oval platter and the wine glasses. She picks up the box. She carries it to the door. Her left knee clicks on the first step going down.
